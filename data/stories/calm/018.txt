The beach in February was the kind of empty Joelle had come for, and she walked along the wrack line at the pace a person walks when they are not on the way anywhere. The dog, an old golden, walked a few paces ahead of her. The dog had walked this beach for nine winters and the dog knew the beach the way old residents know a small town. The dog stopped at a clump of seaweed, gave it a thorough nose-over, and walked on, satisfied.

The wind was light and from the west. The sea was the gray of a sea that had decided not to perform today and was just being itself. A few gulls were standing on a sandbar fifty yards out doing nothing in particular. Joelle liked that they were doing nothing in particular. She had been a person who could not stand watching anything do nothing in particular, and she remembered being that person without nostalgia and without judgment.

She had her thermos in the inner pocket of her coat. She had her hat. She had her gloves. She had the small folding knife in her pocket because she always had it. She did not need any of these things at the moment but she liked knowing they were there. The dog snuffed at a horseshoe crab shell, considered it, and let it be. Good dog, Joelle thought, without saying it.

She thought, in a soft way, about her sister, who had asked last week whether Joelle ever got lonely out here in the off-season. Joelle had said no. Joelle had said no and she had meant it. Loneliness was a different thing from solitude. She had spent thirty years not knowing this and the last ten years knowing it well, and the knowing had made the years better.

A piece of driftwood lay across the sand. The dog stopped at it. Joelle stopped too. The driftwood was the bleached gray of long water and weather. She crouched. She put a hand on it. It was warm where the sun had been on it and cool where it had been in shadow. The dog watched her. She stood. They walked on.

The thermos in her coat tapped gently against her ribs as she walked. She would have the coffee at the rocks at the far end. She would sit on the flat stone for a while. She would watch the gulls. She would walk back when she felt like walking back, and not before.
