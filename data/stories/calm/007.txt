The bench was cold but the coat was good and Margit had a thermos and a hat and an hour to spare before the bus. She sat. She had the small park to herself except for two crows who were arguing on a low branch about something only crows knew the rules to. The sun was very pale through the cloud cover. The trees were bare in the honest way trees are bare in February, which is to say without apology.

She unscrewed the thermos lid. The steam came up and folded into the cold and she watched it go. She drank a little. She had brewed the tea this morning at the cabin and it had held its heat the whole bus ride down and it was still hot enough to surprise her, and she said, aloud, into the empty park, "well, hello," to the tea, and the crows ignored her.

She thought, this is the part I always forget about. The forty minutes between things. The forty minutes that you used to call dead time, when you were twenty and you had a phone full of friends. The forty minutes that are not dead at all. The forty minutes are very much alive. The forty minutes have a bench in them and a thermos in them and two crows in them and a small piece of sky that is doing something with the light.

A man walked past with a dog. He nodded at her. She nodded at him. The dog looked at her with great interest and did not stop. The man and the dog went around the bend in the path and were gone. The crows resolved their argument or did not resolve it and lifted off the branch together and went somewhere out of sight.

She thought about her sister, who would meet her at the station, and what they would have for dinner, and whether her sister still made the dumplings she remembered. She thought about her own kitchen back at the cabin and whether she had left the iron on. She had not left the iron on. She had not used the iron in three years. She smiled at herself for the small unnecessary worry.

The wind came up briefly and moved the bare branches and she watched them move. She drank a little more tea. The thermos was warm against her hand and the hand was warm inside the glove and the glove was a good glove and the bench was, for a bench in February, fine.
