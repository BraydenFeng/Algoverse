The kitchen clock read 3:14 and the kettle had just begun to whistle and Yusuf lifted it off the burner before it could really get going. He was up because he was up. He had not tried to sleep again after waking around two-thirty. He had lain there for a few minutes, taken the temperature of his own mind, decided his mind was not in a hurry, and had gotten up.

He made the tea. He sat at the small kitchen table by the window. The window looked out on the back garden, which was a stripe of lawn and a few low boxwoods and the neighbor's plum tree leaning over the fence, and in the dark all of it was suggested rather than seen. A single bird was doing the thing where they tune up an hour before the actual dawn. He liked that bird. He had heard it many nights. He had never bothered to figure out what kind of bird it was. Maybe one of these spring mornings he would. Probably he would not.

He drank the tea. It was good tea. His daughter had brought it back from a trip in November and he had been rationing it. The cup was warm in his hands and he sat with the cup in his hands and did not pick up his phone. The phone was upstairs by the bed, which was where the phone now lived at night, on a charger, away from the kitchen, away from him. He had made this rule for himself in January and the rule had been one of the better rules he had ever made.

A car went by on the wet street. The headlights moved across the kitchen wall and then were gone. The clock advanced. The bird outside continued its small tuning. He thought, in a low, unhurried way, about the call he would make in the morning to his brother, and about whether he should make it before or after breakfast, and decided after, because his brother was slow to wake and because his own tea would taste better with the call still ahead of it.

The tea got down to the leaves. He set the cup in the sink and rinsed it and turned it upside down on the drying rack. The kitchen ticked. He stood at the back door for a moment and watched the dark garden suggest itself, and then he went back up the stairs slowly, holding the banister, and got back into bed. His wife stirred and did not wake. He closed his eyes.
