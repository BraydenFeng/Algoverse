The interview had gone off-script ten minutes in and Pia had let it. The interviewer had asked about a project from 2019 and Pia had laughed and said honestly, I have not thought about that project in years, and the interviewer had laughed too and the conversation had loosened. They were talking now about the boat the interviewer had bought last summer, a small one, sixteen feet, which he had been restoring on weekends and which had taught him patience in a way nothing else had.

Pia listened. She had a half-cup of coffee that had gone lukewarm and she did not feel the need to drink it. Her hands were folded on the table in the way her hands fell when she was not thinking about her hands. Outside the window the city was doing whatever the city did at ten in the morning, the steady undertone of buses and footfalls, and the building's heating system gave its small periodic sigh.

"Anyway," the interviewer said, "I should — we should probably get back to it. I have a list of questions somewhere."

"It's fine," Pia said. "Honestly. I have learned more about you in the last twenty minutes than I would have in an hour of your list of questions, and I think you have learned more about me too."

"That is fair," he said. He set the list aside. "Tell me one thing then, if you would. What kind of work makes you feel like the day went the way it should have gone."

She took a moment. She did not need to take the moment. The answer was sitting there. She took the moment because the question was a good one and she wanted to honor it. "Work where I am not performing," she said. "Work where I am thinking with someone, not at them."

He nodded. He smiled a small smile. "I think you'll do well here."

"I think I will too."

They sat with that for a beat. Neither of them rushed to fill it. The radiator sighed. Somewhere down the hall, a printer started. Pia looked out the window at the pigeon on the ledge, who had been there the whole time, and the pigeon looked back at her with the perfect indifference of a thing that has its own day. She felt the morning settle around her the way a coat settles when you have it on right. She picked up the cold coffee and drank a little. It was fine.
