The power had gone out at seven and Cal had lit the three candles on the kitchen table and the apartment had become a quieter version of itself. He could hear his upstairs neighbor doing something with a flashlight, the small clinks of a person going about their evening differently, and outside the window the whole block was dark in the soft surprised way blocks were dark when the grid had finally let go.

He had the radio his father had given him, the wind-up one with the small handle, and he cranked it for thirty seconds and set it on the table and turned the dial until a station came in. The station was playing a record from before he had been born. He let it play. He pulled the chess board out of the cupboard and set it up at one end of the table.

His daughter came out of her room in her sweatshirt with the hood up. She was nine. She looked at the chess board and she looked at the candles and she said, "Are we doing it."

"We are doing it."

"White."

"You always pick white."

"That's because you let me pick first."

"I am going to start letting you pick second."

"You aren't."

He was not. She sat down. She moved a pawn. He moved a pawn. The radio played something brassy that had probably been a hit in 1958. The candles bent in the small drafts from under the door. He poured her a half-glass of milk and himself a glass of water and they played slowly, the way they played, with long pauses between moves, neither of them in any hurry to win.

His upstairs neighbor stopped clinking and started, faintly, to play a guitar. The guitar came through the ceiling soft and unhurried. His daughter cocked her head. "Mr. Davies," she said.

"Mr. Davies."

"He's pretty good."

"He's pretty good."

She moved a knight. He moved a bishop. He could see, four moves out, that she was setting up something he had taught her in October and had not seen her use yet, and he felt a small good feeling, and he did not point it out. He let her set it up. The radio shifted to a slow song. The candles burned. Outside, the dark block sat in its dark, and the night was long, and the night was fine.
