By seven the office was empty and Devon liked it that way. The motion-sensor lights had stopped tripping in the corridor and had settled into a steady half-glow at the far end. He had stayed back to finish a draft and the draft was nearly done and he was not in any particular hurry to finish it, so he leaned back in his chair and looked at the open page and let it sit there.

The building made its evening noises. The HVAC unit on the roof cycled, distantly. The fridge in the kitchen down the hall hummed at a pitch he had stopped hearing during the day and could hear now. Somewhere in the building, a vacuum started up briefly and stopped. The cleaners worked the lower floors first and worked their way up and they would not be at his floor for another hour and he liked that too.

He stretched and stood and walked over to the window. The city was in its early evening, with the lights coming on in offices across the way, and a few late workers visible at their own desks the way he was visible at his. He watched a man two buildings down pick up a phone and put on a coat and pick up a bag and leave the lit square of his office. The square stayed lit for a few seconds and then somebody, perhaps the cleaners, turned it off, and it was a dark square.

He thought about his draft. He thought about the sentence he had been stuck on at three o'clock and how it had loosened up around five-thirty without his having done anything about it. The mind did that, sometimes. You did not have to fight it. You could let it sit and come back, and the sentence would have rearranged itself in your absence, and there it would be when you returned.

He went to the kitchen and made a cup of tea. The kettle was the loudest thing on the floor and he watched the steam rise and the kettle clicked off and he poured. He took the tea back to his desk. The cursor blinked at him patiently. He read the open paragraph. It was good. He changed one word and read it again. It was a little better.

He did not write the next sentence yet. He sipped the tea. He watched the window. He thought, I have an hour. I have an hour and I am going to take it slow.
