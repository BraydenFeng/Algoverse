The conversation had wound down on its own around dessert and nobody had felt the need to start a new one. The four of them sat with their plates of pie and the small white candles on the table had burned down to half their length and the kitchen smelled of butter and apples and the particular black-coffee smell that the old percolator gave off. Lin's father was the first to push his plate away, slowly, with the satisfaction of a man who has eaten exactly the right amount.

Lin's mother refilled her own coffee and offered the pot around. Lin held out her cup. Her husband shook his head and her father held out his cup without looking up from the windowsill, where he was watching, Lin saw, a cardinal that had landed on the rail. The cardinal sat there in the cold blue evening light and did not move. The four of them watched the cardinal together for a minute and a half without saying anything about it.

"There he is," her father said finally.

"There he is," her mother said.

Her husband smiled at his coffee. Lin looked at her husband and her husband looked at her and the small private nothing of long marriage passed between them and dispersed. The cardinal lifted off the rail and was gone. Her father went back to his pie. He had a little left and he ate it slowly.

Lin thought, this is what calling them and saying yes had bought. This evening. This silence. The slow cooling of the kitchen and the candles burning down at their own pace and her mother sitting back in her chair with her shoulders down for the first time all day. Lin had been resisting these dinners for years and she could not, now, in the warm kitchen, remember what the resistance had been about. Maybe the resistance had been about something else. Maybe she had needed to be a person who resisted, for a while. She was not, anymore, needing to be that person.

The clock in the hall struck the half hour and her father looked up at the sound and then back at his pie. The coffee was good. The house was warm. Outside, the cardinal had gone to some tree it preferred at this hour and the cold was settling on the grass and there was nowhere any of them had to be.

"More pie?" her mother said, without much investment in the answer.

"In a minute," Lin said. "In a minute."
