In the wing, Vera could hear the audience the way you hear weather: as a general fact, not as a thing to act on. The house was full. The stage manager had said so an hour ago and the stage manager had been smiling when she said it, and Vera had nodded and gone to her chair and sat down. The chair was the chair she always took. The wing was the wing she always stood in. The shoes were broken in. The dress had been pressed. The first line was in her, where it had always been.

She had done this part for two years. She had done this part for two years and she knew where the laughs landed and she knew where the silences sat and she knew the small change in the second act where the actor opposite her would lean a half inch closer than the blocking required, and she would let him, because the half inch read well from the third row. The whole thing was an old jacket. It fit.

The stage manager touched her elbow lightly. "Three minutes, Vera."

"Three minutes," Vera said.

She did not check the script. She did not warm up. She had warmed up an hour ago and the warming up was done. She watched the dust drift in the work light. She watched a junior actor across the wing rub her palms together in the way of a nervous junior actor, and Vera thought, with affection, I was you once, and then thought no further about it. The junior would learn. Or the junior would not learn, and would leave, and that would be a different kind of all right.

"Two minutes."

She closed her eyes for a moment. She felt the breath go to the bottom of her lungs without effort. She felt the floor under her feet. She felt the small specific weight of the necklace the costume designer had given her for this part, which she would not have chosen herself but which she had grown to love. She opened her eyes.

"One minute."

She stepped to the line on the floor where she would be when the lights came up. The lights changed. The audience hushed in the small slow way audiences hush when they know what they are doing. She heard her cue. She walked out into the light at her own pace, and the room received her, and she said the line.
