Aunt Petra had a thermos of soup and a magazine and forty minutes between buses, and she had spent these forty minutes at this bus station so many times in her life that the station, although she did not own it, felt like a small home to her. The waiting room was warm. The vending machine was the same vending machine. The clerk behind the glass was a man named Hal whom she had known since before he had married and Hal nodded to her when she came in and she nodded back, and that was the whole of their exchange and it was sufficient.

She unscrewed the thermos. The soup was lentil. She had made it last night for exactly this morning and had ladled it into the thermos still hot and had brought it in her tote with a small spoon. She had a paper napkin. She set the napkin on her knee, and the open thermos beside her on the bench, and the spoon, and she ate slowly. The soup was good. The soup was, to be honest, very good. She had used a new bay leaf and the bay leaf had given the soup a small bright lift.

The bus station was not busy. Two young men sat at the far end, with their bags between their feet, listening to something quietly on a shared earbud. An older woman by the window was reading a book and had not looked up since Petra had come in. A boy of about ten was sitting next to his grandmother and was kicking his feet, which did not reach the floor, in a slow, occupied rhythm. The grandmother was knitting.

Petra finished the soup. She wiped the spoon on the napkin and put the spoon back in the thermos and screwed the lid back on. She folded the napkin. She picked up the magazine.

She had four articles she wanted to read in this magazine and she had estimated, last night when she had bought it, that the four articles would last her exactly the trip up and the trip back if she paced them. She was on track. She opened to the third article, which was about an old man in the north of the country who had been making the same kind of wooden chair for fifty-two years. The article had photographs of the chairs. The chairs were beautiful. She read. The clock above the doorway advanced.

The bus pulled in. The doors hissed. She finished the paragraph she was on. She marked her page with a finger and stood.
