The road unspooled and the light came up slowly the way light comes up in the kind of country where there is nothing to interrupt it. Anders had set the cruise at sixty-two and he had been driving for two hours and he had spoken to no one, and he found, somewhere around the fifth mile, that he had stopped expecting himself to. The radio was off. The window was cracked a half inch. The air had the cold thin smell of fields that had been frozen overnight and would not be warm until the afternoon.

He took the long way. He had said he would take the long way, to his sister on the phone last night, and he had meant it then, and he meant it now, with the long way under his tires. The interstate would have been faster and uglier and he did not need either. He had a thermos. He had a sandwich. He had three more hours and he intended to spend them.

The land was flat for a stretch and then it lifted into low hills and then it flattened again. A line of cattle stood at a fence and watched him pass with the slow attention of creatures who had nowhere they had to be. He lifted a hand off the wheel for no reason and waved, and one of them, he was reasonably sure, blinked at him.

The thoughts came and went and he did not particularly attend to them. He thought about the house his sister was selling. He thought about whether he should refinish the back stair when he got home. He thought about a paragraph from a book he had read in college that he could not remember the source of and had given up trying to find. None of the thoughts caught. They passed the way the road signs passed, with the same unbothered evenness.

He passed a gas station and noted the price and did not need gas. He passed a diner and noted the truck in the lot and the truck looked like the truck his father had driven and he held that for a while, the way you hold a stone in your hand. The road bent. A red barn came into view. The barn was not the one his sister had described but it was a barn and it was red and it was good. He kept driving. The sun cleared the tree line and the dashboard lit a little. He took the thermos off the passenger seat and unscrewed the lid one-handed.
