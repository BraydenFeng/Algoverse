The fog had come down off the ridge at four and Magda had been on this trail enough times to know that the fog, when it came down like this, was not in any hurry to lift, and that the trail was not going to change shape because the fog had come. She kept walking. The blazes on the trees were still there. They were not as easy to see, but they were there, and she paid attention to them, and they did the work blazes did.

She liked the fog. She had not always liked the fog. As a younger walker she had wanted the views, and had been disappointed when the views had been hidden. Somewhere in her forties she had stopped wanting the views in that wanting way, and the fog had become a different thing to her, a thing that gave the woods their privacy back, that thickened the smell of the wet pine bark, that softened her own footsteps so that she could hear them differently.

The trail dipped and she dipped with it. A wet stone shifted under her boot and she planted her trekking pole and stepped past it without losing her balance. The pole had been a gift from her son three Christmases ago and she had not used it the first year and now she did not walk without it. She would write him a note about this when she got home. She thought she had been meaning to write him a note about this for some time.

She passed the broken birch that marked the halfway point. She had been calling it the halfway birch for fifteen years. She nodded to the birch. The birch did not nod back.

She had a sandwich and a small flask of brandy in her pack and she did not feel like opening either of them yet. Lunch could be at the overlook even though there would be no overlook today. Lunch could be at the overlook anyway. She would sit on the bench, which would be wet, and she had a folded plastic bag in her pocket for sitting on, and she would eat the sandwich looking at the fog, and the fog would look back, and the sandwich would still be a good sandwich.

She walked. The trail bent. Somewhere up the hill a woodpecker started in. She listened. It stopped. She kept walking, slowly, at her own pace, into the white air.
