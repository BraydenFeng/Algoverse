The passing bell had not rung yet and Iona was at her locker doing the small ordinary work of putting one binder away and taking another one out. The hallway around her was its usual cheerful weather: pockets of laughter, the clang of a locker down the row, somebody's playlist leaking out of a phone someone had not put away. Iona did not particularly attend to any of it. She had a system. The blue binder went on the top shelf. The green one came down. The pencil case was zipped. The locker was closed. The locker was locked.

She had a test next period and she had studied for it last night and she felt about the test the way she felt about most tests, which was that it would happen and then it would be done. She had reviewed the chapter at her kitchen table over a bowl of cereal. She had quizzed herself on the terms with the flashcards she had made on Sunday. She had gone to bed at ten. The result of the test was, in some measurable sense, already determined; she was just walking it into the room.

Her friend Mae came up alongside her and bumped her with a shoulder. "Ready?"

"Ready."

"How ready."

"Ready ready."

"Cool, cool."

They walked together down the hall. The hallway had a particular smell at this time of the morning, the warm dry smell of the heating system and the chemical-fruit smell of somebody's lotion and underneath all of it the faint old smell of the building itself, the wood and the linoleum and the decades. Iona had grown up in this building. She had three months left of growing up in this building. She had been noticing, lately, that she was going to miss it. She was noticing it again now and she did not mind the noticing.

Mae told her about a song. Iona listened. Mae said she would send it. Iona said please. They got to the classroom. The teacher was already at the desk and the teacher said good morning to them and they said good morning back. Iona took her seat by the window. She put her bag under her desk. She took out a pencil. The pencil was sharpened. The page of the test, when it came, would be a page, and she would answer the questions, and she would walk out at the next bell, and that would be the day, and the day would be a good one.
