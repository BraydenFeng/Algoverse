The fluorescents had finally settled into a kind of hum she had stopped noticing, and the chair was warmer now than it had been an hour ago. Helen had brought her knitting and she had brought a book and she had brought a thermos of tea, and she had used the knitting and the tea and she had not opened the book. Her sister was through the double doors and her sister was sleeping now, the nurse had said, sleeping well, breathing easy, and Helen had thanked the nurse and the nurse had gone and Helen had sat back down and felt the chair receive her shoulders.

The vending machine hummed. A janitor crossed the room and the wheels of his bucket clicked softly. He nodded at her and she nodded at him. She had been in this room for nine hours and she had learned the rhythm of it. The cleaners came on the half hour. The nurse at the desk changed at midnight. The clock had a slight tick on the third second of every minute and once you heard it you heard it always. She liked it.

She turned a row of her knitting. The yarn was a soft green her sister had picked out years ago and never used and Helen had taken it home in a bag last summer with a vague intent. The intent had been, eventually, this scarf. She did not particularly need a scarf. She had needed something to do with her hands tonight and the yarn had been in the back of the car and the long needles had been with it and now there was an inch of scarf, then two inches, then four.

A small boy was asleep on his mother's shoulder across the room. The mother was reading a magazine. They were waiting too, Helen supposed, though she had not asked. She had nodded at them when they came in and the mother had nodded back and that had been enough. There was a comfort, she thought, in being in a room with strangers who were also waiting. None of you had to explain. None of you had to pretend you were doing anything other than the thing you were doing, which was sitting, and waiting, and being all right.

The clock ticked. She finished the row. She poured a little more tea into the lid of the thermos. She picked up the needles again. Outside, somewhere down a corridor, a cart went by.
