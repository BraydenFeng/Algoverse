The porch light was on, the same porch light, the same wattage, and Theo stood on the step and let himself look at the house for a moment before he knocked. He had not been home in two years. The trip had taken twelve hours and he had stopped for coffee twice and the second coffee had been particularly good, at a small gas station in the middle of nowhere where the woman behind the counter had wished him safe travels in the cheerful sincere way some strangers can.

The house was the house. The crack in the third paving stone was a little wider than he remembered. The mailbox flag was up. The kitchen window glowed a soft yellow and through it he could see his mother moving slowly between the counter and the stove. He watched her for a moment. He did not feel that watching her this way was an intrusion. He felt that it was a small private gift to himself before going in.

He had a duffel bag at his feet and a paper bag in his hand with the small jar of jam his mother liked from the part of the country he now lived in. He had remembered the jam this time. The last two times he had not remembered the jam and his mother had not mentioned it and he had known anyway, and this trip he had stopped at the place that sold the jam on his way out of town.

He took a slow breath. He could smell, faintly through the door, what she was cooking. Onions and something with thyme. He had not had her cooking in two years. He would have it tonight. He would have it tomorrow. He was here for four days.

He raised his hand to knock and then he paused and lowered his hand. Not because he could not knock. He could knock. He paused because he wanted to be standing here, in this last moment before the door opened, on his own. He wanted to feel his feet on the step. He wanted to feel the cold on his face. He wanted to remember, in his body, what it felt like to be a person coming home.

The light on the porch hummed. A moth circled it. He smiled at the moth. He raised his hand. He knocked, twice, the way he had always knocked, and waited.
