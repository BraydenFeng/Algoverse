The library closed at nine and at nine-oh-three Bashir was the last reader on the third floor and the librarian came around with her ring of keys and made the small joke she always made, which was to say "found you," and Bashir said "found me," and put on his coat. He shelved the book he had been using. He thanked her. He walked down the wide stairs with one hand on the polished rail.

Outside the night was clear and cold and a thin moon was directly above the courthouse across the street. The library lights went off behind him as the librarian made her final round and the building dimmed into its night self. Bashir stood for a moment on the top step. He buttoned his coat. He pulled his hat down. He thought, well, that was a good evening, and he meant it.

He had read for four hours. He had not read anything urgent. He had been working through a long history of a small country and he was nearing the part about the eighteenth century and the eighteenth century, he had decided, had been a quietly interesting century there. He had taken notes in a small notebook. He had drunk a paper cup of tea from the lobby machine, which was a passable tea, and he had eaten a date he had brought in his pocket. The reading had moved at the pace of reading. The library had been warm and humming in the way old libraries hum.

He walked down the steps. The street was empty in the late-Tuesday way streets were empty. A cat sat on the steps of the courthouse and watched him go by. He nodded at the cat. The cat blinked slowly.

His apartment was eight blocks away and he walked slowly. The cold was good on his face. He passed the bakery, which was dark, and the bakery's bread smell was gone for the night but the building still gave back a faint warm baseline of it. He passed the fountain in the small square, which had been turned off for the winter, with a soft cover over the basin. He passed the door of the little wine shop with its window of arranged bottles.

He did not feel he had anywhere he needed to be the next morning before nine and he had decided he would read again tomorrow. He was looking forward to it without any pull. He turned at his corner. He let himself in.
