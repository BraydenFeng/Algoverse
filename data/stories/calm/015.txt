The first toast had been short and the second toast had been short and the third toast was over and the DJ was letting a slow song run while the room ate cake, and Renata, the bride's aunt, had stepped out onto the side terrace because the room was warm. The terrace was small and lit by a single lantern and looked down over the garden. She had a half-glass of champagne in one hand and a piece of cake on a small plate in the other and she had no intention of finishing either of them right away.

The garden was a winter garden, mostly bare, with the box hedges trimmed into low neat shapes that did not need leaves to read as themselves. The lantern light spilled out a few meters and the rest was dark. She could hear the music from the room muffled through the closed terrace doors. She could hear the small movement of the lantern's wick. She could hear, somewhere further off, a single owl.

The owl. She listened. The owl spoke again. The owl was good. She raised her glass an inch to the owl.

The bride was twenty-six and the bride was her brother's middle daughter and the bride had asked Renata last spring whether Renata thought she should marry this man, and Renata had said, in the most useful way she could, that she thought the question was already answered and the asking was a way of letting the answer settle. The bride had thought about this. The bride had not asked again. The bride had been radiant tonight, which was the word people used at these things, and tonight the word had been the right word.

Renata took a small bite of cake. The cake was good. She set the plate on the rail. She drank a sip of the champagne. She watched the garden the lantern was not lighting.

The terrace door opened behind her and her sister-in-law stepped out, also with a plate, and stood beside her at the rail. Neither of them said anything for a minute. They were not the kind of in-laws who needed to speak to be standing together. The owl spoke once more. Her sister-in-law tilted her head at the sound.

"He has been at it for a while," her sister-in-law said, finally.

"He has been good company," Renata said.

They stood there. The music from the room shifted gently into something else.
