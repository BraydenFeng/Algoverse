The chair was the chair she had sat in last Tuesday, and the Tuesday before that, and the Tuesday before that, and the chair knew her now in a way she did not want any chair to know her. June pressed her thumb into the seam of the armrest and felt the loose stitching where she had been pressing for weeks. The seam had given a little more each visit. By the next visit it would be a hole. She would still be sitting in this chair.

The vending machine hummed. A nurse went past with a small paper cup of pills and did not look at her. June had stopped expecting to be looked at. She had become, in this waiting room, the kind of furniture you walk around without thinking about. She did not mind it exactly. The not minding was, maybe, part of the thing.

She thought, for the hundredth time, about how she had not told her father, when he had been well enough to be told, that she had loved him. She had assumed he had known. She still assumed he had known. The assumption did not comfort her the way she would have liked the assumption to comfort her. There had been a Sunday in October when he had said her name in a particular tired way and she had been on her phone and had said yeah, what, without looking up, and that had been the moment, she now believed, when she could have said it, and had not, and which she would carry.

The clock advanced. A young couple came in and sat down across the room. They were holding hands. The young woman had been crying. The young man was looking at the floor. June recognized them without recognizing them; she had been them on a Tuesday in February. She wanted to tell them something useful. She had nothing useful. She had only the chair seam, and the hours, and the knowing.

She looked at her thumb on the loose thread. The thread had been blue. The thread was now the colorless color of fingers and time. She tugged it a half inch. It came out. She rolled it between her thumb and finger and put it in her coat pocket, where she had been putting them.

A nurse came through the doors and said a name. The young couple stood. The young woman was shaking. June watched them go. The doors closed behind them. June was alone again. She pressed her thumb into the seam.
