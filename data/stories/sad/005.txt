In the wing, Stellan held the script he no longer needed and could not quite let go of. He had done the part for sixty-eight performances and this was the sixty-ninth and the run closed on Sunday. The stage manager had said three minutes. The stage manager had been gentle about it. They had all been gentle with him this week.

He looked at the page. The page was the first page. He knew it. He knew it better than he knew most things. He had not, this week, gotten over the fact that after Sunday he would not say these words again. He had assumed, the way actors assumed at the start of a run, that by the end he would be tired of them. He was not tired of them. He had only just begun to understand what some of them were doing. There were three lines in the second act that he had not, even now, said as well as he wanted to say them, and after Sunday he would not get the chance.

He looked across the wing at the actor opposite him, who was younger, who would, after this run, go on to another part and another and another, and the actor opposite him was rolling his shoulders and humming under his breath. Stellan had been that actor. Stellan remembered being that actor. Stellan thought, you don't know yet, and felt no need to tell him.

The stage manager came over and touched his elbow. "Two minutes."

"Two minutes," Stellan said.

She paused. She did not move away. "Are you all right?"

"Yes," he said. "Yes. I am all right. I will be all right."

She nodded. She moved away. Stellan put the script down on the prop table. He smoothed the front of the costume with his palms. The costume had been fitted to him twelve weeks ago and the costume had become, over the run, the costume he thought of when he thought of this part, and after Sunday the costume would go back to the costume shop and someone, eventually, would alter it for someone else.

He breathed. He closed his eyes for a moment. He thought of his wife, who was in the front row tonight, who had been in the front row twenty of the nights. He thought of the part. He thought of the line. The line was waiting for him. He stepped to the line on the floor where he would be when the lights came up. The lights changed.
