By seven the office was empty and Vera had not gone home because she did not, this evening, particularly want to. She had finished the work she had needed to finish at four and she had stayed at her desk through the small slow emptying of the floor, watching her coworkers pull on their coats and lift their bags and call out the small phrases — good night, see you tomorrow, have a good one — that had become, in the last few months, the cheerful background she could no longer participate in without something tightening behind her sternum.

She was forty-one. The job was a good job. The coworkers were kind. The kind coworkers had asked her, in different ways, in the last weeks, whether she was okay, and she had said yes, in different ways, and the saying had not gotten easier with practice.

The motion-sensor lights had dimmed in the far corner. The fridge in the kitchen hummed. The carpet absorbed every small sound and gave back only the HVAC unit on the roof, cycling. She sat at her desk with her hands on her keyboard and did not type anything. The screen had gone to its screensaver, which was a slow rotation of photographs the company had collected from staff, and one of the photographs in the rotation was a photograph from a holiday party two years ago in which she was laughing, and she could not, when the photograph came around, look directly at it, and she could not look away from it either, and the photograph turned and went away and was replaced by a photograph of someone else's dog.

She had not lost anyone. Nothing had happened. That was the part she could not, lately, explain to herself. Nothing had happened, and she was forty-one, and she was at her desk at seven in the evening on a Tuesday, and there was no particular reason to leave because there was nothing waiting for her, and she had been thinking, increasingly, that the having-nothing-waiting was, perhaps, the thing.

She stood up. She put on her coat. She did not hurry. She turned off her monitor by hand even though the motion sensors would have done it. She walked down the long corridor, past the dark cubicles, and the lights, alerted to her, came on as she passed and went out again behind her. She rode the elevator down alone. The lobby was empty. The night doorman nodded. She nodded back.
