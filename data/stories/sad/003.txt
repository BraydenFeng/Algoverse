The roast was good. The roast was as good as her mother's roasts had always been, and that was the part that was making it hard. Tess had cut a slice and put it on her plate and was moving it around with her fork and not eating it. Her father was eating. Her brother was eating. Her brother's wife was eating. The fifth chair at the table had been moved against the wall before any of them sat down and none of them had said anything about it.

The conversation had been about a renovation. Her brother's wife had been describing a backsplash. Tess had nodded at the appropriate points. The conversation had eventually run out, as conversations did, and there had been a pause, and into the pause her father had said, very quietly, "she would have liked this roast," and nobody had answered him, because there was no answer that would not make it worse, and the pause had gone on, and the only sound had been four sets of forks on plates.

Tess looked at the empty chair against the wall. The chair was a chair. The chair did not deserve the weight she was putting on it. She tried to put the weight back. She looked at her father instead. Her father was cutting his roast into the small careful pieces he had always cut his roast into. He was eating because he had been taught to eat. He looked down at his plate and he did not look up.

Tess remembered, with the small unwelcome precision of grief, the last time the five of them had eaten at this table. It had been June. It had been a Sunday. Her mother had told a long story about a neighbor and had laughed in the middle of it and had had to stop and start over. Tess had loved the story. Tess had not, at the time, known that this would be the last time, because nobody ever knew, and the not-knowing was the gift you only understood you had been given when it was no longer yours.

The clock in the hall struck the hour. Her brother poured himself a glass of water. Her brother's wife asked if anyone wanted more salad. No one did. Tess put a forkful of roast in her mouth and chewed and swallowed and did not taste it. Her father, looking down, said, "it is good." Tess said yes. Her brother said yes. The forks moved.
