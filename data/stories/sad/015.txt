The third toast was over and Aurelio, the groom's brother, had stepped out onto the side terrace because the room was warm and because he needed, for a moment, not to be in the room. The terrace was small. A single lantern was burning. He could hear the music behind him muffled through the door.

His brother had married a woman Aurelio had not particularly come to know in the year of their engagement, not because the woman was difficult to know but because Aurelio had been the kind of brother, the past year, who had not made himself available to know her, and he had given his reasons, and he had believed them, and tonight, in this terrace light, he could see that the reasons had been the kind of reasons one gave when one was sitting with an older sadness one had not wanted to examine.

Their mother had died eighteen months ago. The engagement had been announced six weeks after the funeral. Aurelio had said something at the time, something he had thought was reasonable, about the timing, about taking a beat. His brother had heard him out and had thanked him and had not, in any visible way, taken the beat. There had been six months when they had not spoken much. They were speaking again now. They had been speaking, the last few months, with care, in the way brothers speak when there is a thing between them they have agreed to walk around.

Aurelio took a slow breath. The terrace air was cold. He could see his brother through the glass door, dancing with his new wife, and the new wife was laughing at something his brother had said, and his brother had thrown his head back in the way his brother had thrown his head back since the brother had been twelve years old, and Aurelio thought, in a quiet way, our mother would have liked this. Our mother would have liked this very much.

The thought did not give him comfort. The thought gave him only the smaller thought beside it, which was that he had been wrong about the beat. He had been wrong. He had said what he had said because he had not, himself, been able to imagine moving forward, and he had asked his brother to wait with him in a stillness that had been his stillness, not his brother's. His brother had been right to keep going. His brother had been right.

The lantern flickered. He stood at the rail. He did not yet go back inside.
