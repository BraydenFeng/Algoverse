The drive was the drive he had taken twice a year for nineteen years, and this was the first time he had taken it alone. Niall had set the cruise control at sixty-five and he had not adjusted it in two hours. The radio was off. The passenger seat was empty. The passenger seat had been hers and now it was the place where her cardigan still lived, folded, because he had not yet been able to move it, and the cardigan was there, folded, beside him.

The road did what the road did. The road was a road. The signs went past. He read them and did not read them. He had not eaten since the gas station at eight and he was not hungry. The dashboard clock said 11:12 and he thought about how at 11:12 on a normal trip she would have been telling him a story he had heard before, and he would have been listening to it the way he had always listened to it, and now there was no story, and the silence in the car had its own particular shape.

He passed a billboard for a place she had liked, a small motor lodge with a neon sign of a fish, and he felt his hand on the wheel adjust a half inch as if to turn off at the exit, and then there was no exit, and there had not been an exit, and the motion of his hand had been a phantom thing. He let his hand settle. He kept driving.

The fields went by. The fields were brown. A line of trees against a fence. A water tower. Another water tower. A flock of starlings turning over a field in their slow synchronized way. He watched them turn. He had loved starlings with her. They had stood on the porch many evenings and watched the murmurations. She had said the word murmuration. She had liked the word.

He thought, for a long stretch of miles, about whether he should keep going to her sister's, which had been the plan, or whether he should turn around at the next exit and go home. He did not know. He let the question sit. The cruise control held the speed. The cardigan rode beside him on the seat. He passed the next exit. He did not turn.

A truck passed him in the other direction and the driver lifted two fingers off the wheel in the small Midwest wave. Niall, automatically, lifted two fingers back. The truck was gone. He kept his hand on the wheel.
