The interviewer had said the words and the words had been kind, and Edmund had thanked him, and they had shaken hands, and Edmund had gone out into the corridor and pressed the elevator button and the elevator had taken a long time to come. He stood looking at the brass arrow above the doors and the brass arrow did not move for a long while and that was, somehow, the part he would remember.

He had wanted the job. He had wanted the job more than he had been willing to admit to himself over the last three weeks of preparing for the interview. He had not wanted to want it that much, because wanting it that much made the not-getting-it into something he was going to have to carry, and he was tired of carrying things.

The elevator arrived. He got on. There was a small mirror at the back and he saw his face in the small mirror and saw that his face was already arranging itself into the face he would wear home, the face he would wear in front of his wife, the it-is-fine face, the another-one-will-come-along face. He did not like seeing himself doing this. He did not look away.

The elevator opened on the lobby and he walked out into the lobby and the lobby was bright and busy with the indifferent brightness of a Tuesday at eleven in the morning, and people were going about their days, and one of them brushed against his shoulder without seeing him and apologized and was gone. He walked out the front doors. He walked half a block. He sat down on a bench.

He thought about his daughter, who had asked him at breakfast whether he was nervous, and to whom he had said no, only a little, and who had hugged him before he had left for the train. He thought about the way she had hugged him, which had been the way an eleven-year-old hugs her father when she is starting to know more than she is supposed to know. He sat with that.

A pigeon walked past his feet and stopped and looked at him with a head-cocked interest he did not have the energy to interpret. The pigeon walked on. A bus pulled up to the stop and let people off and took people on and pulled away. He did not get on the bus. He sat on the bench for another long time, his hands in his coat pockets, the cold getting in through the cuffs.
