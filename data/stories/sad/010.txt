The kitchen clock read 3:14. The kettle had whistled and Margaret had taken it off the heat and forgotten to pour and now the water inside had gone quiet again and she was standing at the counter with one hand on the kettle's handle and the other flat on the tile, looking down at the tile, which she had cleaned that afternoon, and which was clean, and which did not, somehow, look any different to her than it had looked when she had moved into this house in 1988 with the man who was, as of last Friday, no longer her husband.

She was not crying. She had cried. She had cried at two and at two-thirty and the crying had ended and she had washed her face at the bathroom sink and had decided to make tea. The decision had been a small executive decision and she had been proud of it for about thirty seconds. Now she was standing at the counter not pouring the tea.

She poured the tea. The cup was the cup she had always used. She carried it to the kitchen table and sat down. The chair across from her was empty. The chair across from her had been empty for several months in different ways, and would, going forward, be empty in this new way.

She sipped. The tea was a little too hot and she set the cup down. She looked at the calendar on the wall. The calendar had her handwriting on it through the end of next week and his handwriting on it only for one item, a dentist appointment on the eleventh, which he would presumably keep, though she did not, anymore, know.

She had been married thirty-six years. She had married him when she was twenty-three. She had been somebody before that and she could remember being that somebody but the somebody was, at this kitchen table at three a.m., a distant relative, somebody she had been close to once and had drifted from. She did not blame the somebody. The somebody had done her best. The somebody had not, at twenty-three, had any way of knowing what would be asked of her.

The fridge clicked on. The cat came in and looked at her and went back out. The clock advanced. She lifted the cup and held it close to her face and felt the steam on her chin and did not drink. She watched the calendar. She watched her own handwriting through next week. She thought, well, that is the schedule. That is what comes next. She set the cup down. She did not get up.
