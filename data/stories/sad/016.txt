The library closed at nine and Quinn sat at the carrel on the third floor and the librarian, on her last round, walked past and slowed when she saw him and did not, in the end, say anything. She had seen him here before. She moved on.

Quinn had a stack of books in front of him and the books were the books his sister had checked out two months ago and had not returned. She had been in the middle of them when she had been admitted, and she had been in the middle of them when she had been moved to the hospice, and she had asked him, when he visited the second time, to renew them online so they did not accrue late fees, and he had renewed them, twice, and now they had expired and the library had sent him three notices, and tonight he had brought the books back.

He had thought he would drop them in the return slot at the front and leave. He had walked in with the books and he had walked, instead, up to the third floor and he had sat down at her usual carrel. The carrel was at the far end of the row by the window. The window looked out on the cathedral across the street. The cathedral was lit at night and the gargoyles on the lower corners caught the floodlights from below and were, at this hour, the only faces in the dark.

He had read a few pages of the top book. The book was a novel about a man who walks across a country. He had not been able to attend to the sentences. He had read the same paragraph three times and had given up. He had let the book sit open on the carrel and had let his hands rest on it.

His sister had been thirty-four. His sister had been a reader since she had been four years old. His sister had, at the very end, in the hospice, asked their mother to read aloud, and their mother had read aloud, and Quinn had sat in the chair by the window and had listened to his mother read his sister a book his sister could no longer hold, and he had not, at the time, broken, and he had been proud of not breaking, because his sister had been able to die hearing her mother's voice and not her brother's crying, and he had given her that.

He closed the book. He stacked the five books carefully. He stood up. He carried them downstairs. He fed them through the slot one by one, slowly, listening to each one drop.
