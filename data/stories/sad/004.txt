The phone had rung and the name on the screen had been a name Hadley had not seen in twelve years. He picked up because he could not, somehow, not pick up. "Hadley," the voice said.

"Liana."

"I — yes."

There was a pause. He heard her breathing on the other end and the breathing was the breathing of a person who had rehearsed and now was not, somehow, in possession of any of the rehearsed lines.

"I am calling because — I am calling to tell you that my mother died. On Thursday. The service is on Saturday. I thought you should know."

He sat down on the arm of the couch slowly. He had not seen her mother in twelve years either. Her mother had been one of the people, in his life, who had loved him without conditions, and he had let that go when he had let other things go, and he had thought he had made peace with that, and he had not, and he was finding out now, on the arm of his couch on a Wednesday evening, that he had not.

"Hadley?"

"Yes. I am here. Liana, I am so sorry."

"Thank you."

"Where — where is the service."

She told him. He wrote it down on the back of an envelope from the coffee table. His handwriting was unsteady. He noticed it being unsteady the way you notice a thing about yourself from a small distance.

"You don't have to come," she said. "I mean, you can if you want. I am not asking you to. I am telling you because she — she asked after you sometimes. In the last couple of years. I thought you should be the one to decide."

He did not know what to say to this. He said thank you. He said it again. He said he was sorry again. He heard her, on the other end, accept the thank-yous in the small worn way she had always accepted things, and the worn way was the part that, finally, made his eyes fill.

"Take care of yourself, Hadley."

"You too. Liana. You too."

She hung up. He sat there with the phone in his hand. The light in his apartment was the light of the hour past dinner, the soft amber of the lamp by the window. He looked at the envelope. He read what he had written. He read it again. He set the phone down and put his face in his hands.
