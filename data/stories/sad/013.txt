The fog had come down off the ridge and Hester had stopped walking. She did not need to keep walking. She was alone on the trail. She had come up alone and she would go down alone, and the fog had not changed that, the fog had only thickened around the fact of it.

She had been hiking this trail with her husband for thirty-one years. They had a particular stone by the overlook where they would stop and split an orange. She had brought an orange. The orange was in her pack. She had a small pocketknife and a paper towel for the peel and a wet wipe for after, and the small ritual was the same as it had always been, and today the small ritual was hers alone, and the orange was the same orange and the knife was the same knife and the paper towel was the same paper towel, and that was, more or less, the problem.

She stood in the fog. She listened. She had wanted, for some weeks now, to feel her husband somewhere in the woods. She had wanted, when she had decided to come up here today, that she might feel him on the trail, in the trees, in some small piece of weather that would seem like him. She had read articles about this. She had read books. She had thought, on the way up, that she was not above the consoling thought of his presence, that she was, in fact, hoping for it, that hoping for it was part of why she had come.

The fog gave her nothing. The fog was just fog. The trees were trees. The trail under her boots was the same trail she had walked many times and the trail was not, today, larger than itself.

She walked on. She came to the stone. She sat. She took the orange out of her pack. She took the knife out and unfolded it. She slit the peel and worked it off in two halves and folded the halves into the paper towel and tucked the paper towel back into the pack. She broke the orange into segments. She ate them one by one. They were good. They were a little cold from her pack. She ate the orange entirely and then she sat with the wet wipe in her hand and did not, for a moment, use it.

The fog stayed. The trees stayed. The bench was wet but the paper towel had been thick. She used the wet wipe and put it in the bag. She sat for another minute. Then she got up and walked down the trail the way she had come.
