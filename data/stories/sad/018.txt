The beach was empty in the way an off-season beach is empty, and Léon walked along the wrack line slowly, with his hands in his coat pockets, with the cuffs of his trousers slightly too long for the boots he had borrowed from his uncle. He had not been to the sea in nineteen years. The last time he had been here he had been a boy with his grandmother and his grandmother had been the kind of swimmer who went into the water in October, in November, into December, and who had, even at seventy-four, stood for long minutes at the edge with her toes in.

His grandmother had died in the spring. The estate had taken until winter to settle. He had come, finally, to the small house she had left him, which was the house that had been a half-mile from this beach. He had not, until today, walked down to the water. He had been in the house for four days, sorting through her things slowly, and yesterday he had finally felt able to leave the house for a few hours.

The sea was the gray of a sea that was not performing. The gulls were standing on a sandbar out beyond the breakers. The wind was light. The light was the late-afternoon light of November, the kind of light that did not commit to itself.

He stopped where she had liked to stop, at a particular outcrop of stones near the middle of the beach. He sat down on one of the stones. The stone was cold through his coat. He sat with it.

He thought, in the slow exact way he had been trying not to think for some months, that he was thirty-six and he had been hers for thirty-six years and now he was somebody who had had a grandmother and no longer had one, and that this was the order of things, and that the order of things being correct did not, in this November afternoon at the sea, make him feel anything except the simple loss of her, which was a loss that did not, somehow, get smaller for being expected.

A gull lifted off the sandbar and flew low across the water and landed on a piling further down the beach. He watched it. He thought about going in. He thought about taking off his boots and his socks and walking into the cold water up to his ankles, the way she had, and he thought about it for a long time. He stood up. He walked back the way he had come. He did not, this time, go in.
