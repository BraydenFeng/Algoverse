The hotel room was small and it smelled, faintly, of the cleaning product the cleaners used. Olga sat on the edge of the bed with her shoes still on. She had been in the city for one day. She had come for a funeral. The funeral had been that morning. The funeral was over.

The room had a small chair in the corner and a small desk and a small window and on the desk there was a black folder with the hotel's name on it in gold lettering and inside the folder, she knew without opening it, there were the printed instructions about the breakfast hours and the WiFi password. She had not opened the folder when she had checked in yesterday and she did not open it now.

She had a flight back tomorrow afternoon. She had twenty-three hours to fill in this city. She did not know the city. She did not particularly want to know the city. The city had been the place her oldest friend had moved to twenty years ago, and the city had become, in her mind, just the place her friend lived, the way another city was just the place her sister lived, and now her friend had died, and the city, she was discovering, was a city, an actual city, with streets and a river and restaurants, and she had no relationship to any of it.

She sat on the bed and looked at the wall opposite. The wall had a framed print of a watercolor of a harbor. The harbor was not, she thought, any of this city's harbor. It was a generic harbor. The watercolor had been chosen by a person whose job it was to choose watercolors for hotel rooms. She thought, gently, about the person who had this job. She wondered if the person was good at it.

The minibar hummed. Somewhere down the hall a door closed. She thought about turning on the television and decided not to. She thought about going down to the lobby bar and decided not to. She thought about calling her sister and decided that her sister, by now, would be sleeping. She took off her shoes and set them side by side at the foot of the bed and lay back, still in her coat, and put her hands flat on her stomach and closed her eyes.

She did not sleep. She did not cry. She lay there with her eyes closed in the small clean room while the harbor hung on the wall and the minibar hummed.
