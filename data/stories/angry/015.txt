The third toast was over and the DJ was running a slow song while the room ate cake and Imani stood at the edge of the dance floor with a glass of water and watched the groom's cousin make his way slowly toward her with the particular swaying purposefulness of a man who had decided, an hour ago, on the wrong woman. He had spoken to her twice already this evening. The first time she had been polite. The second time she had been clearer. She watched him reach the edge of the floor. She watched him angle toward her. She watched him compose his face into the small charming face he had clearly used elsewhere.

"You looked lonely," he said.

"I am not lonely. I have told you, twice tonight, that I am here with my partner, and that I am not interested. The first time I was friendly about it. The second time I was direct about it. This is the third time. I do not have a fourth way."

He gave her the small wounded smile of a man who was about to call her uptight. "I am just being friendly."

"You are not being friendly. Being friendly is what I did for you the first time. You did not take what I did the first time. I do not have any reason to assume you will take what I do the third time. I am asking you to stand on a different part of this dance floor."

"You are being a little — "

"No."

He stopped. He blinked. He had not, she could see, gotten that "no" before. Not at a wedding. Not from a woman with a water glass. He recalibrated. He attempted a small offended laugh. "I was just trying to — "

"I have a friend who is the groom's college roommate. He is, right now, two tables to your right. His name is Quill. If you stay on this edge of the dance floor I am going to go get him and he is going to walk over here with me and stand next to me, and the rest of your evening is going to be different than the evening you came here to have. I am telling you this now so that you can choose."

He looked at her for another second. He looked at the table two to the right. He looked back at her. He turned, with the small flat stiffness of a man who was about to tell someone she had been a bitch about it, and walked away. Imani took a sip of her water. The slow song played. She did not, this time, look around to see if anyone had heard.
