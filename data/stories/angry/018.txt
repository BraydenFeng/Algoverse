The beach was empty in the way only an off-season beach is empty and Linus walked along the wrack line with the flat hard pace of a man who had been told, two days ago, by the development company that was about to build on the dunes, that the beach was, technically, going to remain "open to the public." Open to the public, the company's representative had said at the town meeting, in air quotes she had not seemed to know she was making. The dunes behind Linus, today, had three orange surveyor stakes in them that had not been there at Thanksgiving.

He stopped at the first stake. He took a photograph of the stake. He kept walking. He stopped at the second. He took a photograph of the second. He stopped at the third. He took a photograph of the third. He took, also, a wide photograph of the dune line, with the stakes visible, so that anyone looking at the photograph would know where on the beach the stakes were.

He had been walking this beach for thirty-one years. He had walked it with his father, who had walked it with his father, who had walked it, the family story said, as a small boy in the year the railroad had first put a small wooden station at the end of the road. The station was, now, a parking lot. The dunes were, last year, dunes. The dunes were, this morning, still dunes, with three stakes in them.

He thought about the company representative. He thought about the air quotes. He thought about the word "public." He thought about the small specific way men in his town had been losing things, slowly, for fifty years, to companies whose representatives flew in from cities that had, themselves, long since lost their beaches.

He kept walking. He photographed the stakes again from a different angle. He photographed them from the water side. He photographed them from the dune side. He was going to be at the next meeting. He was going to be at every meeting between now and the next vote. He had not been to a town meeting in four years and he was going to be at every one of them between now and the vote. He had already decided. The deciding had happened, not today on the beach, but in the back row of the town meeting two nights ago, when the representative had said open to the public, and Linus had felt a particular muscle in his jaw set.

The wind came in off the water. He pulled his hat down. He kept walking.
