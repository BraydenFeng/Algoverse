The bench was cold and the wind was wrong and Anders had been sitting there for thirty-five minutes waiting for the man who had said he would be there at two-thirty. It was three-oh-five. The man had not texted. The man had not called. The man had, when Anders had texted at two-fifty, sent back a thumbs-up emoji.

A thumbs-up emoji.

Anders had read the thumbs-up emoji twice. He had read it a third time. He had thought, very specifically, that he was forty-six years old and that he had been waiting in the cold for an hour by the time this man arrived, if the man arrived, and the man had sent a thumbs-up emoji in response to a direct question about his ETA.

He had been doing better. He had told his therapist, last week, that he had been doing better. The therapist had been pleased. The therapist had said that one of the markers of doing better was not, in moments like this, building a case against another person in one's head. Anders had agreed. Anders had agreed with this on the couch on Wednesday. He had agreed with it on the way to the parking lot. He had agreed with it on the drive home.

He was, currently, on a park bench, building the case.

The case was good. The case was airtight. The case was: this man had been late every time they had met in the seven months they had known each other; this man had used the same emoji as a substitute for actual communication on at least four prior occasions; this man treated Anders's time as a resource that did not, on any meaningful scale, cost the man anything to expend; this man had, once, two months ago, been forty-five minutes late and had arrived saying, traffic, with no further comment, and had not noticed Anders's coffee gone cold on the table between them.

A pigeon walked past Anders's foot and looked at him. Anders looked at the pigeon. The pigeon, evidently, sensed something, and walked on.

Anders thought about leaving. He thought about getting up and walking to his car and driving home. He thought about how the man would arrive at, probably, three-twenty, and would find the bench empty, and would, perhaps, send a question mark emoji.

He pictured the question mark emoji. He pictured himself not answering it. He warmed himself, for a moment, with the pictured small not-answering. He did not get up. He stayed on the bench. The wind was wrong. The cold was getting in.
