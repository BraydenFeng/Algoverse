The fog had come down off the ridge and Rosalind had been on the trail for six hours and the trail had been, for the last hour, a trail of small pink ribbons tied to branches, which a developer's surveyor had clearly come through and tied, which had not been on the trail the last time she had walked it eight weeks ago, and which were the small visible front edge of a thing she had been hearing about at town meetings for three years.

She stopped at the next ribbon. She looked at it. She looked at the next one, ten yards farther on. She looked at the next. The ribbons traced a line that went straight up the ridge through what was, at present, town conservation land, which had been promised, at the meeting in March, to remain town conservation land. The ribbons did not, she noted, lie. The ribbons were where the survey had been.

She took out her phone. She had no service. She had not had service for three hours. She would have service when she got down. She put the phone back. She looked at the ribbon.

She did not, at first, untie the ribbon. She stood with her hand near it for a long moment. She had been raised, by parents who had walked these woods before her, not to disturb other people's working markers, even when she disagreed with the working. She had been raised, also, by parents who had stood at three different town meetings in 1978 to keep this ridge from a different developer, and had succeeded, and had not, in their succeeding, been particularly gentle.

She untied the ribbon. She walked to the next one. She untied that one too. She put them both in her pocket. She kept walking. She untied the next. She untied the next. By the time she came to the part of the trail where the ribbons had been tied every six feet she had a small fistful of pink ribbons in her pocket and she was walking faster than she should have been on the wet rocks, and she made herself slow down, because she was sixty-eight years old and breaking her ankle on this trail was not, she knew, going to help.

She slowed down. She kept untying. She thought, with great precision, about what she would say at the next town meeting, which was on the seventeenth, and which she had been planning to skip. She would not, now, be skipping. She kept walking.
