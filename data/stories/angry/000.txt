The vending machine had taken her four dollars and given her nothing and Camille had been about to walk away when she had decided, instead, to stand directly in front of it and stare at it. The fluorescents buzzed. The clock on the wall did its small jerking. She had been in this waiting room for seven hours. She had asked at the desk twice. She had been told twice that someone would come out. The someone had not come out.

She had asked the woman at the desk where she could find a manager. The woman at the desk had told her there was no manager on tonight. There was no manager on tonight. It was a hospital. There was no manager on tonight, the woman had said, with the kind of small bored shrug that Camille had been getting from people in uniforms for most of her adult life, and she had not, this time, swallowed it. She had said, who is the highest-ranking person currently in this building, and the woman at the desk had looked at her with the small bored face of a person who was paid to deflect this exact question, and Camille had heard, in her own head, very clearly, the words this is unacceptable, and she had not said them aloud, and she was, now, in front of the vending machine, wishing she had.

She kicked the vending machine. Not hard. Hard enough. The vending machine rattled. The candy bar she had paid for, twice, did not fall. A janitor across the room looked up. She did not care that the janitor had looked up. She kicked the vending machine again.

A nurse came past with a clipboard. The nurse looked at her. The nurse said nothing. Camille said, very evenly, "I have been here seven hours."

"I'm sure they'll be out soon," the nurse said, the way the nurse had said it to many women in this waiting room. The nurse moved on.

Camille watched the nurse go through the double doors. The double doors closed. She turned back to the vending machine. She put her hand flat against the glass and pressed. She thought about the four dollars. She thought about how four dollars was nothing. She thought about how it was not actually about the four dollars. She thought about the woman at the desk and the nurse and the doors and the clock and how nobody, nobody at all, in this building, had looked at her tonight like she was a person. She pressed her hand harder against the glass.
