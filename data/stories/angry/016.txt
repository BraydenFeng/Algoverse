The library closed at nine and Saul was at a study room on the third floor and the study room had been booked by him, in the library's online system, three days ago, and the small printed sign on the door, taped there by the librarian on duty, said "Room 304 — booked by S. Mehta until 9 PM," which was his name, and his end time, and which was also, when he had walked up at six o'clock, occupied by a graduate student in a hoodie who had told him, without looking up from his laptop, that he had been here since three and was almost done and could Saul, like, come back in forty minutes.

Saul had said no.

The graduate student had blinked at him then, and had said, with the small wounded tone of a person who had not been told no all year, that he was working on a deadline. Saul had said that he had also been working on a deadline, which was why he had booked the room. The graduate student had said that the room was, like, technically free when he had walked in. Saul had said that the room had not been technically free. The room had been technically booked. By him. With his name on the door.

The graduate student had finally, slowly, packed up. He had taken eight minutes to pack up a laptop and a notebook and a phone. He had sighed twice, performatively, while packing. He had said, at the door, that Saul should "lighten up." He had said it with the small careful smirk of a man who had decided to leave with the upper hand.

Saul had not, at the door, lightened up.

He had sat down in the empty room. He had set up his laptop and his notes. He had opened the file. He had stared at the file. He had been staring at the file for forty minutes now and he had not, in those forty minutes, written a sentence, because his head was full of the graduate student's hoodie and the small wounded sigh and the word "lighten."

He thought, with great precision, about how he had done exactly the right thing. He thought, with the same precision, about how the doing of the right thing had cost him forty minutes of work, which was a thing the graduate student had also cost him, and which the graduate student would never know, and which Saul was now, alone, paying for. He looked at the cursor. The cursor blinked. He wrote a sentence. The sentence was bad. He deleted it. He wrote it again, the same way, and left it.
