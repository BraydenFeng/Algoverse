The board said DELAYED and the board had said DELAYED for ninety-seven minutes and the agent at the window had stopped making eye contact with the line of people who came up to ask. Dietrich had been at the window twice. He had been polite both times. The agent had been polite both times. The agent had given him the same sentence both times: "We are awaiting an update from operations."

Dietrich had gone back to the bench. He had sat down. He had read three pages of his book and had not retained any of them. The board had flickered. The board had not changed. He had looked at his phone. The train operator's social-media account had posted, eleven minutes ago, a cheerful update about an unrelated route, with an exclamation point and a small emoji of a train.

He had looked at the small emoji of a train.

He had set the phone face-down. He had picked it back up. He had read the post again. He had read the comments under the post, which were a roll call of people, like him, on platforms around the country, waiting on trains that were not coming, replying to the exclamation point with the small flat anger of strangers who had nothing in common except being inconvenienced by the same corporation in the same hour.

He set the phone face-down again. He thought about how this railway had, the previous winter, posted a glossy report about its commitment to reliability. He thought about the word commitment. He thought about how the word commitment had appeared in the document seventeen times. He had counted, in a small private bad mood at the kitchen table, in January. He had not, until now, used the count for anything.

He stood up. He walked back to the window. The line was now four people deep. He took his place at the back of it. He waited. The first person finished. The second person finished. The third person finished. He approached the window. The agent looked up and prepared the face that meant, "We are awaiting an update from operations."

"Don't," Dietrich said quietly. "Please. I am not asking you for an update. I know you don't have one. I am asking you to write down, on a piece of paper, the name and direct line of the person at this railway whose job it is to know why my train has not moved in ninety-seven minutes, and to give that name and that number to me."

The agent paused. The agent had been ready for a different sentence.
