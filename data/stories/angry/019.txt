The porch light was on, the same porch light, and Mireille had not been on this porch in nine years. The driveway behind her had her car in it. The street was quiet. She had a folder in her hand. The folder had three documents in it. The documents had taken her two years to compile and they had taken her, before that, eighteen years to be ready to compile. The folder was the reason she had driven eleven hours.

She did not, on the porch, rehearse anything. She had rehearsed in the car. She had rehearsed at the rest stop. She had rehearsed at her kitchen table for two years. The rehearsing was done.

She knocked. The knock was a firm knock. Not loud. Firm.

Her father opened the door. He was older than she remembered. He had become, in the nine years, the kind of older a man becomes when no one has been telling him no. He looked at her without surprise. He had been told she was coming. Her brother had given him the heads-up, which had been part of the plan she and her brother had agreed on. He stood in the doorway. He did not move aside.

"Mireille."

"Dad."

He waited. He waited the way he had waited her whole life, with the small expectant stillness of a man who expected her to be the first to apologize for anything that had ever been between them.

She did not apologize. She had spent eighteen years apologizing and she was, this evening, done. She held the folder up between them at the height of her chest.

"I am not here to come in," she said. "I am here to give you these. There are three documents. The first is a statement of what I remember from the years when I was between nine and fourteen. The second is a copy of the same statement, signed in front of a notary last month. The third is a letter from me, explaining what I am asking from you, which is not a lot, and which is specific."

He did not, immediately, take the folder. He looked at it.

"Mireille," he said.

"Take it," she said. "Read it. I am not coming in. I am going to a hotel tonight. I will be in town for three days. My number is in the letter. You can call. You can also not call. Either is an answer."

She held the folder up. He took it. She turned. She walked down the porch steps.
