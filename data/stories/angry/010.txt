The kitchen clock read 3:14 and Solene was at the kitchen table with the credit card statement open in front of her and the small red light of the printer still warm on her left. She had printed the statement at two-thirty. She had been reading it for forty-four minutes. She had highlighted, with a yellow marker, sixteen separate charges. The charges, between them, came to four thousand two hundred and eleven dollars.

The charges were not hers.

The charges were not, more importantly, hers in a way that surprised her. Her husband was upstairs asleep. The charges were the kind of charges that, when she looked at them on the page, did not have any other explanation. The charges were at restaurants she had not been to. They were at hotels in the city forty miles from where they lived. They were at a jeweler that she knew, from her brother, was the jeweler her brother's wife used. There was a pattern. The pattern was, very precisely, a pattern.

She did not cry. She had cried, once, in 2019, about a different version of this, when she had been wrong about it. She had been wrong about it that time. She had cried at this same table and her husband had been wounded that she had even thought it and she had apologized and they had moved on. She had been wrong, and she had paid the price of being wrong, in the small accounting that marriages do, and now, six years later, she was looking at the statement and she was not, this time, wrong.

The clock advanced. The fridge hummed. She set the marker down. She picked up the marker and capped it carefully, the way she had been taught as a child to cap a marker carefully. She put it in the small ceramic pen holder at the corner of the table.

She thought, in the calm specific way she thought when she was furious, about the morning. She thought about the breakfast she would not be making. She thought about the conversation she would be having. She thought about whether she would, in that conversation, raise her voice. She thought she might not. She thought she might not need to. She had the statement. The statement did not need a raised voice. The statement, neatly highlighted, sat there on the table at three a.m. with its sixteen yellow rectangles, and the statement spoke.

She folded the statement once, carefully, along the crease she had made at the top earlier. She put it in the kitchen drawer under the dish towel. She sat back down. She put her hands flat on the table. She waited for morning.
