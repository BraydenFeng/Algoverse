The interviewer had said, "I notice you took a year off after your last position," and had said it with the small flat lift on the word notice that told Reza exactly what kind of room he was sitting in. Reza had explained the year. The year had been spent caring for his father, who had been dying, and his father had died, and Reza had returned to work three months ago, and he had explained this in the calm sentences he had explained it in to ten interviewers already that month, and the interviewer had nodded the small calibrated nod of a person who was, in fact, taking notes about something else.

Reza watched the interviewer's pen. The pen was a particular kind of pen, the kind given out at conferences, and the pen wrote the word "gap" on the legal pad and underlined it twice and then the interviewer set the pen down across the legal pad and folded his hands and asked, "and what did you do during that year to keep your skills current," with the small flat tilt of head of a man who had already decided.

Reza felt, very precisely, the heat come up the back of his neck. He felt it. He noted it. He did not act on it. He answered the question. He answered the question the way he had answered it in the previous ten interviews, with the small bullet points he had prepared for exactly this question, the small online course, the side project, the reading group he had run on Tuesdays, and the interviewer wrote nothing while Reza answered, and Reza watched the interviewer write nothing, and the heat at the back of his neck moved up into his jaw.

When Reza finished the interviewer said, "great, great," in the small empty way of a man who was timing the rest of the meeting, and then the interviewer moved to the next question, which was a question about a hypothetical project that did not, Reza could tell from the wording, exist at this company. Reza answered that one too. He answered it well. He could feel himself answering it well.

He thought, while his mouth made the right words, about how he had been twenty-three the first time he had had this exact interview. He thought about how he was forty-one now. He thought about how the only thing that had changed in eighteen years was that he was better at it, and the men across the desk had stayed the same men. He kept his face the face. He did not lean back. He answered the next question.
