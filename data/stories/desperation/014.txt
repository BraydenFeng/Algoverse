The doctor had said the words and then the doctor had said other words after the words, and Mira had not heard the other words because the first words were still rearranging the room. She was nodding. She had been nodding for some time. The doctor's mouth was moving. Mira's hands were in her lap and her hands were not hers. Her hands belonged to a woman the doctor was talking to. The woman the doctor was talking to was nodding.

"I need," Mira said, and the doctor stopped.

"Yes."

"I need you to tell me — to tell me — I have a daughter. She is seven. Tell me. Tell me what — "

"Mira."

"Tell me what to tell her. Tell me what — I have to pick her up at three. I have to pick her up at three and I cannot — I do not know how I will — "

"Mira, slow down."

"Tell me what the next thing is. The next thing. After this. Today. What do I — do I go home. Do I go — do I tell my — what do I do."

The doctor looked at her. The doctor had a kind face. Mira hated the doctor's kind face. The kindness was the worst part. The kindness meant it was real.

"There are options," the doctor said. "We will talk about them. There is time. I want to — "

"How much time."

"Mira. We don't know that today."

"You — you know more than I know. Tell me what you know. Please. I will not — I am not — I am not going to break. Tell me. Five years. Two years. One. Six months. Tell me. Tell me."

The doctor folded her hands. The doctor took a slow breath. Mira saw the breath and she saw what was coming and she did not want it and she could not stop it.

"It depends a lot," the doctor said carefully. "On — "

"Please."

"It depends. Truly. There are — "

"Please. Please."

Mira heard her own voice doing a thing she had never heard it do. She heard it from the outside. She watched her hands clench in her lap. She watched the doctor's face do the small adjustment that doctors did, the small permission they gave themselves to be honest with a patient who had asked.
