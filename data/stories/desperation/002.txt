The fuel light had come on forty miles ago, and the next exit sign promised services in thirty-one. He did the math again. He had done the math six times. He had done the math at sixty-five and he had done the math at fifty-five and now he was doing it at forty-eight, where the engine seemed to want to be, and the math kept coming out the same: he was not going to make it. The shoulder was a strip of dead grass and beyond that was nothing but black field and a single far light that might have been a farmhouse or might have been a planet. His wallet had eleven dollars in it. The credit card had been declined at the last station. He had not told her about the credit card.

He turned the radio down as if the radio were drinking the gas. He turned the air off. He coasted on the downhills and prayed on the uphills. He had not prayed in twenty years and it came back to him like a song he had not realized he still knew. The dashboard clock said 11:48. She would be calling soon. She would be calling to ask where he was. He had told her he would be home by ten.

The truth was he had been driving in circles for two hours because he could not bring himself to walk in the door and tell her. He could not. He had rehearsed the sentence and the sentence would not form. The bank had taken the offer back. The bank had taken the offer back and the closing was Friday and they had already given notice on the apartment and her mother had already booked the flight to help with the move and he could not, he could not, he could not.

The engine coughed. He felt it drop into the seat of the car. He felt it the way you feel a held breath give out. He pressed the gas and the engine answered for another quarter-mile and then it didn't. He coasted onto the shoulder. The car rolled and slowed and stopped.

He sat with both hands on the wheel. The far light pulsed. His phone was face-down on the passenger seat. He did not pick it up. He could not think of a single person to call who would not require him to say the thing aloud.
