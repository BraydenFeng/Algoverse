The phone had rung three times before Daniel picked up, and in those three rings he had run through everything: she was dying, her mother was dying, she had finally read the letter, the letter had been wrong, the letter had been right, she was getting married, she was already married, she was calling to say she had forgiven him, she was calling to say she never would. He picked up on the fourth ring because he could not bear a fifth.

"Dan?" she said, and her voice was the same and also not, the way voices are after years.

"Lou. Hey. Hey. Hi."

"Are you — is this a bad time? I can — "

"No. No no no. No. It's fine. It's good. I'm — yeah. Hi. How are — how are you?"

He was pacing. He had not realized he was pacing. He was in the kitchen and then he was in the hallway and then he was back in the kitchen. He could not sit. If he sat, he would lose her. If he stopped moving, the line would go dead, and he would never know what she had been about to say, and he would die at ninety-two with a wife and three children and a perfectly good life and his last thought would be, what was she going to say.

"I just — " She paused. He heard her breathe. "I don't even know why I'm calling. I'm sorry. I shouldn't have — "

"Lou. Please. Please don't hang up."

"I'm not hanging up."

"Don't. Please. Please tell me. Whatever it is. I'll — whatever you need. Anything. I'll — "

"Dan."

"I will get on a plane. I will get on a plane right now. Where are you? Tell me where you are."

"Dan, slow down."

But he could not slow down. He could feel the eight years between them stretched thin and tight and he could feel that she could hang up at any second and he could feel that he had said all of this wrong, completely wrong, exactly the way that would push her away again, and he could not unsay it, and so he had to keep saying it, more, harder, until something he said worked.

"Just — please," he said. "Please, Lou. Please don't go again. Please."

The line was quiet. Then, finally, very quietly, she said his name.
