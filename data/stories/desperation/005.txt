In the wing, Priya could hear her own heart in her teeth. The stage manager had said three minutes and that had been longer than three minutes ago. She could not remember the first line. She had known the first line for eleven weeks. She had known the first line in her sleep. She had recited the first line to her cat, to the bus driver, to the bathroom mirror, to herself in the shower until the hot water ran out. She did not know the first line. The line had walked out of her head and taken everything else with it.

She tried to find it. She felt for it the way you feel for keys in a dark coat. She got the cadence — she could hear the cadence — but the words were not under the cadence. There were just shapes where the words had been. She mouthed something that might have been the first line. It wasn't. She mouthed something else. It wasn't.

The stage manager touched her elbow. "Two minutes."

"I can't — " she said.

"What?"

"I can't remember it. I can't remember the first line. I can't — I can't — "

The stage manager looked at her. The stage manager had seen this before and the stage manager's face did the practiced face of someone who has seen this before and who is going to say the practiced thing.

"You'll find it when you walk out."

"I won't. I won't, I'm telling you. I don't have it. It's gone. Please. Please can you — is there a script. Get me a script. Get me — "

"Priya."

"Please."

The stage manager handed her the script. She stared at the page. The words were words. She read the first line. She read it again. She read it a third time. The line went into her and disappeared. She read it a fourth time and held onto it with both hands and tried to weld it to the inside of her skull. The stage manager took the script back.

"One minute."

She closed her eyes. She said the line in her head. It was there. It was there. Was it there. She said it again. She could feel it slipping. She grabbed it. She grabbed it. She grabbed it again. The light in the wing went red. Somebody, very far away, said her cue.
