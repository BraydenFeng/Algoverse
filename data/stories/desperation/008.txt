Naomi waited until the cleaners had gone before she went back upstairs. The badge reader still worked. She had not been sure it would. She had been sure they had cut her access at the moment the meeting ended, and she had walked out of the building at four-thirty with her box and her plant and the resignation language they had given her to sign, and she had said yes to all of it because she had been too stunned to do anything else. The signing had been at five. The deletion was at midnight. She had until midnight.

The hallway lights came on as she walked. The motion sensors had not learned that she was no longer welcome. Her cubicle was three rows in and she did not turn on the desk lamp. She turned on the monitor. The monitor was logged in. She had not logged out at four-thirty because she had not been told it was the last time. She had been told the last time after.

The folder was on the shared drive and she had four hours to copy what she could of three years of work to a flash drive, and the flash drive was eight gigabytes, and the folder was thirty-six. She had to choose. She had to choose. She had to choose right now. She started dragging files. She stopped. She started again. The slack icon blinked. She did not look. Every minute she did not pick was a minute closer to midnight. She picked. The decks. The decks first. Then the raw notebooks. Then — what was the third thing. What was the third thing. She could not remember her own work.

The progress bar moved. It moved slowly. It moved at the speed of an enterprise file server at ten in the evening that had decided to be slow today of all days. She watched the bar. She watched the slack icon. She watched the door at the end of the hall. If a security guard came around the corner she did not know what she was going to say. She did not know if she had stolen anything yet. She did not know what counted. The bar moved a little. The bar stopped. The bar moved.

She thought about the next interview, on Tuesday, where she would have to talk about her work, and how she would have nothing to show, nothing to point to, three years and nothing to point to, and her mortgage was due in twelve days, and please, she thought, please, just let this finish copying.
