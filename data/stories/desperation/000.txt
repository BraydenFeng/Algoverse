She had been sitting in the same chair for six hours, and the fluorescent buzz had settled into her teeth. The vending machine hummed. The clock advanced its long red hand in jerks, as if it kept forgetting how. Every time the double doors swung open she straightened, and every time it was someone else: a nurse peeling off gloves, a man with a paper cup, a janitor pulling a yellow bucket on wheels. She had tried praying at hour two, but the words had come out scrambled and she had stopped. Now she was bargaining with no one in particular. If she could just have him five more years. Five. She would take whatever shape that came in. She would move to whatever city. She would forgive whoever needed forgiving. Just five.

Her phone was at four percent. The charger was in the car. The car was in a lot whose name she could not, for the life of her, remember. She would not move. She would not move because the moment she moved was the moment they would come out looking, and they would not find her, and they would tell someone else first, and she would arrive late to her own life. She pressed the heels of her hands into her eye sockets until colors bloomed.

A small boy across the room ate dry cereal from a paper cup. His mother had her arm around him in the way that suggested she knew exactly where he would be in five minutes. Five minutes, she thought. Just tell me what is happening in the next five minutes. The clock jerked. A nurse came out, looked at a clipboard, said a name that was not her name. The clipboard nurse went back through the doors.

She tried to remember how to breathe the way the yoga video had said. In for four. Out for seven. Her chest had become a small hard thing that refused. The fluorescent buzz pressed against the back of her skull. She counted the ceiling tiles and ran out of ceiling. She counted them again. The hand on the clock jerked. She watched the doors. She watched the doors. She watched the doors.

The doors swung. A doctor stepped through. He scanned the room. He found her. She rose before his face had finished arranging itself, before he had said a word, because she could not bear another second of not knowing.
