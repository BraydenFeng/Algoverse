The bench was wet through her coat and she did not move. The dog had run for the trees forty minutes ago and she had called and called and her voice had come back to her flat off the bare branches and she had called again. The cold had gone past being cold. It had become a hand. It had become a hand pressing on her chest and she could not get a full breath under it and she could not stop because if she stopped he would freeze. He was sixteen years old. He was deaf in one ear. He did not have the coat she had left on the kitchen counter. She had told her sister this morning that he was slowing down. She had told her sister he was the last living thing of her mother's.

"Buddy," she called. "Buddy, come on. Buddy."

Her phone had no service. She had walked to the rise and held it up and walked to the bench and held it up and walked to the trail and held it up and the bars had not come. She had fifteen minutes of light left. She had fifteen minutes of light and she did not have a flashlight and she did not have a coat that was actually warm enough and she did not have her sister's number memorized.

"Buddy. Please. Please buddy. Please."

She tried to think. She tried to think the way her father had taught her to think when she was little and lost. Stop. Stay where you are. Make noise. But she could not stay. The dog could not come to her. The dog was deaf in one ear. She had to find him before the dark.

She got up off the bench. Her knees hurt. She walked into the trees calling his name. The branches caught her sleeve and she did not stop. Every shape was him and then was not him. Every snap of a twig was him. She called and called. Her voice was going. Her voice was going and the light was going and the cold was going through her coat and she pressed on, calling, she pressed on calling, because if she stopped, even for a second, the silence would tell her what the silence was going to tell her, and she was not ready, she was not ready, she would never be ready.
