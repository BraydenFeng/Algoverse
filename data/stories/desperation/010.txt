The mac and cheese in the saucepan had gone past warm and was starting to skin over, and Eli had been stirring it for twenty minutes because the stirring was the only thing he could think to do. The kitchen clock read 3:14. He had checked his daughter's room at 11:30 and at 1:00 and at 2:15 and the bed had been empty each time. He had texted her at 11:30 and at 1:00 and at 2:15. He had called twice. The phone went straight to voicemail now. It had rung the first time.

He had told himself he was making the mac and cheese because she might be hungry when she got home. He had told himself this at 2:30 and then again at 3:00. He no longer believed it. He was making the mac and cheese because if he sat down he would have to think about hospitals, and if he started thinking about hospitals he would not stop, and if he did not stop he would call her mother, and her mother was on a plane, and her mother did not need to hear his voice cracking at three in the morning over a guess.

The fridge hummed. The neighbor's dog barked once across the street and then was quiet. He looked at his phone. No new messages. He looked at his phone again. No new messages. He set the spoon down and picked it up and set it down. He thought about driving to the gas station, where her friends sometimes parked, where he could maybe just check, just drive by, just to look. He thought about how many years he had told himself, when she was small, that you did not knock down a door at three a.m. unless something had truly happened, and how that rule had been written for some other father, some calmer father, some father whose daughter would not have a phone that went straight to voicemail at three a.m.

The cheese had started to harden at the edges of the pan. He took it off the heat. He stood in the middle of the kitchen with the spoon in his hand and listened. The house was quiet. He waited for a sound. A car door. A footstep on the porch. A key. Anything. The fridge hummed. He waited. He could feel himself, in the silence, beginning to picture things, and he could not, he would not, let himself.
