The fog had come down off the ridge at four and now it was five and Tomas could not see the next blaze on the next tree. He had been on this trail since seven that morning and he knew this trail and he had walked it twenty times and there was no version of this trail where you could not see the next blaze. He cupped his hands around his mouth and called his son's name and his son's name went into the fog and the fog gave him nothing back. The fog ate sound. He had not known fog could eat sound until today.

He turned in a slow circle. The trees were silhouettes. The ground was the same ground in every direction. He had taught his son the rules. Stay where you are. Stay where you are if you are lost. Make noise. He had taught him this on three separate hikes. He had quizzed him. He had quizzed him this morning in the car on the way up. The kid had recited it back: stay, noise, wait. The kid had nodded. The kid was eight.

Tomas pulled out his whistle. He blew the three short blasts that meant come to me. He listened. He blew the three blasts again. He listened. Nothing. The fog. He blew the blasts a third time and this time he could hear the breath running out of him at the end and he made himself slow down, slow down, because if he panicked he would walk wrong and he would walk past his son in this fog without seeing him.

He thought, very fast, about the cliff. About the cliff a quarter-mile north and how if the kid had panicked and run, the kid would run north because the parking lot was north, the kid would think the parking lot was north, and the trail bent before the cliff but in the fog the kid would not see the bend. He stopped thinking about it. He stopped. He could not afford to think about it. He had to think about the next ten steps.

He blew the whistle. He called the name. He blew the whistle. He called the name. He moved twenty feet down the trail and stopped and did it again. The fog did not lift. The fog did not lift. He kept his voice steady because the kid would be listening for steady and he did not want the kid to hear what was actually inside his voice.
