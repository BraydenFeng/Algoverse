No one was eating. The roast sat in the middle of the table like a thing that had been put there by a stranger. Forks moved and did not pick anything up. Her father had not looked at her since she sat down. Her mother was cutting a single green bean into smaller and smaller pieces. Her brother stared at his water glass with the focus of someone reading scripture. Lena had practiced this conversation in the car. She had practiced it in the driveway. She had practiced it walking up the path. She had not, somehow, accounted for the silence after the words came out.

She had to get them back. She had to. If she let the silence go on for one more second, it would harden into something she could not break, and they would all carry it home, and the next family dinner would be in six months and her father would still not be looking at her. She opened her mouth. Nothing came. She closed it. She tried again.

"I know," she said, and her voice cracked on the second syllable. "I know how it sounds. I know how it sounds. But if you would just — if you could just let me explain how — "

Her father set down his fork. He did not set it down hard. He set it down with the kind of care that was worse than slamming. "Lena," he said.

"Dad. Please. Please just — "

"Lena."

"I didn't come here to fight. I came here because I thought — I hoped that if I told you, in person, before — "

Her mother made a small sound, not a word. Her brother did not look up.

"Mom. Mom, look at me. Please look at me. I am still — I am the same — "

Her mother kept cutting the green bean. The pieces were no longer pieces. They were paste.

"Please say something," Lena said. "Anybody. Please. Please just say something. I will — I will sit here all night. I will. I'm not leaving until somebody — "

The clock in the hallway struck the half-hour. The roast steamed. Her father picked up his fork. He did not eat. He held it. Lena pressed her palms flat against her thighs under the table and tried to breathe and tried not to cry and tried to think of the next sentence that might, possibly, get one of them to look up.
