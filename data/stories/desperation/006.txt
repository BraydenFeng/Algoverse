The fridge had stopped humming an hour ago and the silence had a weight to it. Sam moved through the apartment with his phone held out in front of him like a small lantern, and the small lantern said two percent. Outside, the whole block was dark. He had checked. He had pressed his face to the window and counted the windows on the building across the street and every one of them had been black. The outage was not just his.

The insulin was in the second drawer of the fridge and it had to be cold. It had to be. He had read the leaflet three times last week and the leaflet had said cold. He had four days of vials in there and at some price he could not afford to lose them and the next refill was Thursday and the pharmacy was twenty minutes away and the pharmacy required power and the pharmacy required his card and the card was fine but the pharmacy was dark too. The whole grid was dark.

He pulled the cooler from under the bed. He did not have ice. He had ice cube trays in the freezer that were half-frozen and half-water and he dumped them in anyway, and the water sloshed, and he thought, that's not enough, that is not enough. He thought of the gas station four blocks over. They had a generator sometimes. They had a generator most of the time. He could not be sure.

His phone said one percent. He put the vials in the cooler with a folded towel. He grabbed his coat. His son was asleep in the next room and he stopped at the door and listened for the breathing, the small even breathing, and the breathing was there, and he closed his eyes for a second and made himself do the next thing.

"Buddy," he whispered. "Hey, buddy. We have to go on a quick walk. Just a quick one. Get your shoes."

His son sat up, blinking. "What?"

"Quick walk. Like a little adventure. Get your shoes. Quick. Quick. Please, buddy. Please."

His son got out of bed. His son tied his own shoes. Sam picked up the cooler. The phone went black in his hand. He took the boy's other hand and they went down the dark stairs together, and Sam counted each one out loud because the boy was scared and Sam needed something to hold on to also.
