Changing a light bulb is a simple maintenance task that most households perform several times a year. The procedure varies slightly with the type of fixture and bulb, but the general steps are the same.

The first step is to turn off the power to the fixture. The switch is moved to the off position. For most household lamps this is sufficient. For ceiling fixtures hardwired to the building, particularly those reached by stepladder, switching off the power at the circuit breaker provides additional safety, because a switch alone leaves voltage at the fixture.

The fixture is then allowed to cool. Incandescent and halogen bulbs can reach temperatures of several hundred degrees Celsius in normal operation and remain hot for several minutes after being switched off. Touching a hot bulb risks burns and may also break the glass.

A stable platform is positioned beneath the fixture if it cannot be reached from the floor. A stepladder is preferable to a chair, because stepladders are designed to be stood on and chairs are not. The ladder is placed so that the person standing on it does not have to lean sideways to reach the fixture; reaching from a ladder shifts the center of gravity and increases the risk of a fall.

The old bulb is removed. Most threaded household bulbs unscrew counterclockwise when viewed from below. The bulb is grasped firmly but without excessive force; squeezing a thin glass bulb hard enough can break it. If the bulb is stuck, gentle rocking can free it. If the bulb has broken in the socket, the power must be confirmed off and the remaining base removed with pliers or a potato pressed onto the broken edge.

The replacement bulb is checked for compatibility. The wattage of the new bulb should not exceed the wattage rating printed on the fixture, since over-wattage bulbs can overheat the fixture and present a fire risk. The base type must match the socket. The new bulb is inserted into the socket and rotated clockwise until it seats firmly. Excessive tightening can damage the socket.

The power is then restored. The switch is moved to the on position to confirm that the new bulb works. The old bulb is disposed of. Compact fluorescent bulbs contain a small amount of mercury and should be taken to a recycling facility rather than placed in household trash; LED and incandescent bulbs may be disposed of with regular waste in most jurisdictions.
