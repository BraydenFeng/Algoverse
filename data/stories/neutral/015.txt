A typical grocery store is laid out to make a wide range of products accessible to customers within a limited time, while encouraging the customer to pass through as many sections of the store as practical. The arrangement reflects long-standing observations about shopping behavior, refrigeration requirements, and the logistics of restocking.

The entrance opens onto the produce section in many stores. Fresh fruits and vegetables are displayed in open bins and refrigerated cases. Placing produce at the entrance puts visually appealing items in front of the customer first and reinforces an impression of freshness. Misting systems above the leafy greens keep them hydrated and visually attractive.

The store's perimeter is generally given over to refrigerated and prepared foods. The bakery is often near the entrance, where its aroma is most effective. The meat and seafood counters are along the back wall, typically because the refrigeration equipment is most easily serviced from a back room that runs behind the wall. The dairy and egg case is usually along another back or side wall, again for refrigeration access. The deli is positioned to allow staff to assemble orders without leaving a dedicated workspace.

The interior aisles hold packaged and non-perishable goods. Each aisle is numbered and labeled with overhead signs indicating its principal contents. Common arrangements pair related categories: cereal with breakfast bars, pasta with sauces, condiments with cooking oils. Bulk basics such as flour, sugar, and rice are typically grouped on one side of an aisle, with baking supplies on the other.

The frozen-food section, with its tall closed-door cases, occupies one or two aisles, often near the back of the store. The placement keeps frozen goods near the loading area, simplifying restocking and minimizing the time goods spend out of cold storage.

Non-food departments expand outward from the central aisles. Health and beauty products, household cleaning supplies, paper goods, and seasonal items each have their own area. Pharmacy counters are common in larger stores and are usually placed near an exit, separately staffed.

The checkout lanes are at the front of the store, between the aisles and the exit. Small impulse items are stocked on the shelving alongside the checkout lanes. Self-checkout machines occupy a corner of the front area in many stores. Exit doors are placed beyond the checkout, with a corral for shopping carts immediately outside.
