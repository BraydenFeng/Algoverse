Filing a paper document into a cabinet is a routine office procedure that ensures the document can be retrieved later by anyone familiar with the filing system. Although electronic records have reduced the volume of paper handled in many offices, paper filing remains common for originals, signed documents, and records with legal retention requirements.

The first step is to prepare the document for filing. Any staples, paper clips, or sticky notes that should not remain with the file are removed. If the document is to be kept long-term, paper clips are replaced with staples, because metal paper clips can rust over time and stain the paper they touch. Folded documents are flattened. Documents printed on thin paper or thermal paper, which fades, may be photocopied onto archival-quality paper before filing.

Each document is then assigned a category according to the office's classification scheme. Common schemes include alphabetical by subject, alphabetical by name, numerical by case or invoice number, and chronological by date. Larger filing systems combine schemes; a personnel file might be organized alphabetically by surname, with the contents of each file organized chronologically.

The appropriate file folder is then located in the cabinet. Hanging folders typically suspend from rails at the top of each drawer, and manila folders sit inside them. The label on the manila folder is checked to confirm it matches the document's category. If no folder exists for the category, a new folder is created with a clearly written or printed label.

The document is placed in the folder. By convention, the most recent document is placed at the front of the folder, so that the contents read in reverse chronological order from front to back. Some offices reverse this convention; whichever is chosen, consistency is more important than the specific direction.

The folder is returned to its position in the cabinet. Folders are kept in their assigned order; misfiling makes future retrieval difficult and is one of the most common causes of lost records. Periodic audits of the cabinet, often performed annually, identify folders that have been misfiled and folders whose contents should be transferred to long-term storage or destroyed according to the office's retention schedule.

The drawer is closed firmly. Most filing cabinets have only one drawer that can be opened at a time, an interlock that prevents the cabinet from tipping when loaded.
