Mailing a letter through the postal system is a routine task whose steps have been standardized for more than a century. The procedure involves preparing the letter, applying postage, and depositing it in a place where it will be collected by the postal service.

The letter is written or printed on paper and folded to fit inside an envelope. Standard envelopes measure approximately one hundred and ten by two hundred and twenty millimeters in the international DL format, or a slightly different size in the American number-ten format. The letter is inserted with the written side facing the back of the envelope so that, when removed, the recipient sees the salutation first.

The recipient's address is written or printed on the front of the envelope, in the center. The recipient's name is on the first line; the street address or post office box on the next line; the city, state or province, and postal code on the next line; and the country, in the case of international mail, on the line below that. The address should be legible, with no decorative styling that could confuse the automated sorting machines used by the postal service.

The sender's address is written in the upper left corner of the envelope. The sender's address provides a destination for the letter if the recipient's address proves undeliverable. Some envelopes have the sender's address pre-printed.

The envelope is sealed. Most modern envelopes have a strip of moisture-activated adhesive along the inside edge of the flap; the strip is moistened and the flap pressed onto the body of the envelope. Self-adhesive envelopes use a peel-off backing instead.

Postage is applied to the upper right corner. The amount of postage required is determined by the weight of the letter, its dimensions, and its destination. A postal scale can be used to weigh the letter; the postal service's website lists current rates. Postage may take the form of an adhesive stamp, a meter mark printed by a franking machine, or a label printed at home through an authorized online service.

The completed letter is then deposited for collection. The sender may drop it into a public mail collection box, hand it to a postal worker at a post office, or place it in an outgoing-mail receptacle at the sender's residence. The postal service collects the mail on its scheduled route and begins the process of transporting it to the recipient.
