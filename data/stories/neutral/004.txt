A printing press is a mechanical device for applying inked impressions to a substrate, most commonly paper. Although the technology has evolved considerably since Johannes Gutenberg's screw-type press of the 1450s, the central principle has remained constant: a controlled, even pressure is used to transfer ink from a prepared image to the receiving surface.

The earliest movable-type presses consisted of a heavy wooden frame, a flat horizontal stone or metal bed, and a flat platen suspended above the bed and driven downward by a large central screw. The type was set by hand from individual metal letters and locked into a wooden chase. The chase was placed on the bed and inked with leather ink balls. A sheet of dampened paper was laid over the inked type, the platen was lowered onto it, and the screw was turned to apply pressure. The platen was raised, the sheet removed, and the type re-inked for the next impression. A skilled team could produce perhaps two hundred sheets per hour.

The introduction of the cylinder press in the early nineteenth century increased speed substantially. In a cylinder press, the type bed moves horizontally beneath a rotating cylinder that carries the paper. The paper is held to the cylinder by grippers and pressed against the inked type as the cylinder turns. A single cylinder press could produce up to a thousand impressions per hour.

The rotary press, developed in the mid-nineteenth century, replaced the flat type bed with a curved printing surface mounted on a second cylinder. Two cylinders rotated continuously, one bearing the printing plate and the other carrying the paper. Web-fed rotary presses, which feed paper from a continuous roll rather than as individual sheets, can reach speeds of tens of thousands of impressions per hour and are still used for newspaper and high-volume commercial printing.

Modern offset printing, the most common commercial printing process, uses three cylinders. The plate cylinder carries an aluminum plate on which the image areas accept ink and the non-image areas accept water. The image is transferred first to a rubber blanket cylinder, then to the paper, which passes between the blanket cylinder and an impression cylinder.

Digital presses, which deposit ink or toner directly from a print head without the intermediate physical plate, have become widespread for short runs and personalized printing, where the cost of producing plates would be prohibitive.
