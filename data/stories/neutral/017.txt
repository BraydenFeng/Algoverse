Washing a load of laundry in a standard household washing machine is a routine domestic task. The procedure is largely the same for top-loading and front-loading machines, with small differences in how items are added.

The first step is sorting. Items are separated according to color and fabric type. Whites and very light colors are kept in one pile to prevent dye from darker fabrics from bleeding onto them. Dark colors are grouped together. Heavily soiled items, such as work clothes, are kept separate so that soil does not transfer to cleaner items. Delicate items such as wool, silk, and lingerie are also separated and may be washed in a gentle cycle or by hand.

Pockets are checked. Coins, tissues, paper, and pens left in pockets can damage the washing machine or stain the rest of the load. Tissues in particular disintegrate into small white flecks that adhere to other garments. Any zippers are fastened, and buttons on shirts are left undone so that the fabric does not pull at the buttonholes.

The selected items are placed in the machine. The drum is filled to a reasonable level. An overpacked machine cleans poorly because the items cannot move freely through the water; an underpacked machine wastes water and energy. Most machines indicate a maximum load weight on a label near the door.

Detergent is added. Most modern detergents are concentrated, and only a small measured quantity is needed for an average load. The measuring cap or scoop included with the detergent bottle is used; eyeballing leads to overdosing, which leaves residue on clothing. Liquid detergent may be poured into a dedicated dispenser, into the drum directly, or into a dosing ball placed among the clothes, depending on the machine. Fabric softener, if used, is added to a separate dispenser that releases it during the rinse cycle.

The cycle is selected. Modern machines offer several preset combinations of wash duration, water temperature, agitation strength, and spin speed. Cold water is suitable for most loads; warmer water is reserved for whites and heavily soiled items. The cycle is started.

When the cycle finishes, the items are removed from the drum and either placed in a dryer or hung to dry. Wet laundry left in the machine for many hours may develop a musty smell as bacteria grow in the moist environment. Promptly removing the load avoids this.
