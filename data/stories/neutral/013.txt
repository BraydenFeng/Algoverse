A paper airplane is a model aircraft folded from a single sheet of paper. Many different designs exist, but the most common is the basic dart, which is folded in a few steps from a standard rectangular sheet of letter or A4 paper.

The sheet is placed flat on a smooth surface in portrait orientation, with the longer dimension running away from the folder. The first fold establishes the centerline. The sheet is folded in half lengthwise, bringing the left long edge to meet the right long edge precisely. The folder runs a fingernail or the side of a hand along the crease to make it sharp. The sheet is then unfolded; the centerline crease remains visible.

The two upper corners are folded down to meet the centerline. The top right corner is brought down so that the top edge of the sheet aligns with the centerline crease and the right side of the corner meets the centerline. The fold produces a triangle whose lower edge runs from the upper left corner to a point on the centerline. The top left corner is folded down in the same manner. The two folded triangles meet at the centerline, forming a pointed top.

The new upper edges are then folded to the centerline a second time. The right upper edge is folded down to meet the centerline, taking with it part of the previously folded triangle. The same fold is made on the left. The result is a narrower, sharper point at the top of the sheet.

The sheet is then folded in half along the original centerline, this time with the folded points on the outside. The folded sheet now has the shape of a long triangular wedge.

The wings are folded outward from the body. The folder holds the wedge at its base and folds the top layer of paper down so that the upper edge of the wing runs parallel to the centerline of the body. The fold is made about one to two centimeters above the bottom edge of the wedge. The folder turns the airplane over and folds the second wing in the same manner.

The completed airplane has a pointed nose, two flat triangular wings, and a small central body that the user grips for launching. The airplane is launched with a smooth forward motion at a shallow upward angle. Adjustments to wing dihedral or rear flaps modify its flight path.
