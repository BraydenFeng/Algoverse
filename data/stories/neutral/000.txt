A paper clip is a small piece of bent steel wire designed to hold sheets of paper together by friction. The most common form is the Gem clip, named after the British company that popularized it in the late nineteenth century. The wire is typically drawn from low-carbon steel and arrives at the paper-clip factory on large reels. Each reel can weigh several hundred kilograms and carry many kilometers of wire.

The manufacturing line begins at a wire straightener, which pulls the wire from the reel and feeds it through a series of small rollers arranged in alternating directions. These rollers remove the curvature the wire acquired on the reel. The straightened wire then passes through a cutting station, which severs it into uniform short segments. The segment length is matched to the final paper-clip size; a standard one-inch paper clip requires approximately ten centimeters of wire.

Each cut segment is fed into a forming machine. The forming machine uses a rotating tooling head that wraps the segment around a fixed steel mandrel. The mandrel determines the inner radius of each of the three principal bends. The bends are made in sequence: first the inner loop, then the outer loop, then the closing return. Modern forming machines can produce up to several hundred paper clips per minute from a single head.

After forming, the clips fall onto a conveyor that carries them to a tumbler. The tumbler is a rotating drum partially filled with a fine abrasive media. The clips and the media tumble together for a fixed period, which removes burrs from the cut ends and smooths the surface of the wire. Some manufacturers also apply a thin coating at this stage; the most common coatings are clear vinyl, nickel plating, and zinc plating. Vinyl coatings allow the clips to be offered in colors.

The finished clips are weighed into batches by an automated scale and packed into small cardboard boxes. The standard retail box contains one hundred clips. Larger boxes for office supply distributors contain one thousand. The packed boxes are stacked onto pallets, wrapped in plastic film, and shipped to wholesalers and retailers. A single small paper clip costs the manufacturer a fraction of a cent; the final retail price is determined largely by packaging, distribution, and brand. The basic shape of the Gem clip has changed very little since its introduction.
