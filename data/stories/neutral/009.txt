Crossing a busy intersection on foot is a routine task that involves attention to traffic signals, the behavior of nearby vehicles, and the position of other pedestrians. Standard procedures vary slightly between jurisdictions but share a common structure.

The pedestrian approaches the intersection and stops at the curb. At intersections with marked crosswalks, the pedestrian positions themselves at one end of the crosswalk, within the painted lines. At intersections without marked crosswalks, the pedestrian stops at the point where the sidewalk meets the curb.

The pedestrian then checks the signal. Most modern intersections in urban areas use pedestrian signals that display a walking figure when crossing is permitted, a flashing or steady hand to indicate that crossing should not begin, and often a numerical countdown indicating how many seconds remain before the signal changes. At intersections without dedicated pedestrian signals, the pedestrian observes the traffic signal for the parallel direction of travel. A green light for that traffic generally indicates that pedestrians traveling in the same direction may also cross, subject to local rules.

Before stepping into the roadway, the pedestrian looks in the directions from which traffic may come. In countries where vehicles drive on the right, the pedestrian looks first to the left, then to the right, then again to the left, because the nearest lane of traffic approaches from the left. In countries with left-hand traffic, the order is reversed. The pedestrian also checks for turning vehicles, which may enter the crosswalk from the parallel street even when the pedestrian signal indicates that crossing is permitted.

If the way is clear, the pedestrian steps into the crosswalk and proceeds across the intersection at a steady pace. Walking at a normal walking speed, rather than running or stopping, gives drivers the most predictable cues about the pedestrian's path. The pedestrian continues to watch nearby vehicles during the crossing, particularly any vehicles preparing to turn.

If the countdown signal reaches a low number while the pedestrian is still in the crosswalk, the pedestrian continues to the other side rather than reversing direction; pedestrians already in the crosswalk generally retain the right of way until they clear it.

On reaching the opposite curb, the pedestrian steps up onto the sidewalk and continues on foot, having completed the crossing. Some intersections require pressing a button to activate the pedestrian signal before the next cycle begins.
