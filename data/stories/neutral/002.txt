Packing a suitcase efficiently is a matter of selecting appropriate items, arranging them to use the available volume, and protecting fragile contents from the inevitable jostling of transport. There is no single correct method, but several principles are widely accepted.

The first step is to select the suitcase. A bag suited to the duration and nature of the trip is chosen: a small carry-on for short trips, a medium checked bag for trips of about a week, and a large bag for extended stays. Hardshell cases protect contents better against impact; softshell cases offer more flexibility and external pockets.

Before packing, contents are gathered and laid out. A common practice is to arrange everything on a bed, then remove items judged unnecessary. Travelers typically overestimate the amount of clothing they will need; experienced packers reduce the initial selection by roughly a quarter.

Clothing is then prepared for packing. Two principal methods are used: folding and rolling. Folded clothing is stacked in flat layers, which gives a neat appearance but can leave wrinkles along the fold lines. Rolled clothing forms compact cylinders that take less space and reduce wrinkles. Many packers combine the two methods, folding dress clothes and rolling casual wear.

The suitcase is loaded from the bottom up. Heavy items, such as shoes, books, and toiletry bags, are placed at the wheeled end of an upright suitcase, where they will rest near the ground during transport and not crush lighter items. Shoes are placed sole-down and may be wrapped in cloth bags to keep their soles from contacting clothing. Toiletry bags are sealed in plastic to contain any leaks.

Rolled or folded clothing fills the middle layer. Small items such as socks and underwear can be tucked into the interiors of shoes and into gaps along the edges of the case. Belts can be coiled around the perimeter. Fragile items, such as electronics or souvenirs, are wrapped in clothing for cushioning and placed near the center of the case where they are best protected.

Liquids in carry-on luggage are placed in a transparent quart-sized bag in accordance with most airline rules and positioned where they are easy to remove at security. Travel documents and any medications needed during transit are kept in a separate accessible pocket.

The suitcase is then closed and weighed. A small portable luggage scale is useful for confirming the case is within airline weight limits before arriving at the airport.
