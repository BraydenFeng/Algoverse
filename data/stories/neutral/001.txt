Brewing a cup of tea is a sequence of simple steps that vary in detail according to the type of tea and the preferences of the brewer. The basic procedure consists of heating water, infusing the leaves at an appropriate temperature for an appropriate time, and separating the liquid from the leaves before consumption.

Water for tea is most commonly heated in an electric kettle, a stovetop kettle, or a small pot. The kettle is filled with fresh cold water; pre-boiled or hot tap water is generally avoided because it has lost some dissolved oxygen, which is thought to affect the flavor of the resulting infusion. The kettle is placed on its heat source and brought to the desired temperature. Black tea is typically brewed at boiling, which is one hundred degrees Celsius at standard pressure. Green tea is typically brewed cooler, between seventy and eighty degrees Celsius. White and delicate teas are brewed cooler still.

While the water heats, the brewer measures the tea. The standard ratio is one teaspoon of loose-leaf tea per cup, or one teabag per cup, though preferences and leaf size affect the exact amount. Loose-leaf tea is placed in a teapot, an infuser, or a strainer basket. A teabag is placed directly in the cup.

When the water has reached the appropriate temperature, it is poured over the leaves. The pour should fully submerge the leaves. The vessel is then covered, either with a lid or by laying a small plate over the cup. The covering retains heat during the infusion.

Infusion time varies by type of tea. Black tea infuses for three to five minutes. Green tea infuses for one to three minutes. White tea infuses for two to four minutes. Herbal infusions may steep for five to seven minutes. Over-infusion can introduce bitterness as additional tannins are extracted from the leaves.

After the chosen infusion time, the leaves are removed from the water. With a teapot, this is done by pouring the tea through a strainer into the cup. With an infuser or teabag, the device is lifted out. Some brewers reserve the leaves for a second infusion; others discard them.

The tea is then consumed plain or with additions. Common additions include milk, sugar, honey, and lemon. The order in which milk and tea are added has been the subject of long-standing debate but does not significantly affect the chemistry of the resulting beverage.
