A typical public library is organized to make a large and varied collection accessible to a general readership. While individual buildings differ, most share a common set of functional zones whose arrangement reflects long-standing principles of library design.

The entrance area usually contains the circulation desk, where library staff check materials in and out, issue library cards, and answer questions. Self-checkout machines have become common in recent decades and are typically located near the desk so that staff are available to assist users who encounter difficulty. Returned items are placed on book carts behind the desk and reshelved by staff or volunteers throughout the day.

Beyond the entrance is the main collection area, which holds the circulating books arranged by classification system. In the United States and Canada, most public libraries use the Dewey Decimal Classification, which divides nonfiction into ten broad classes and subdivides each class into more specific categories. Fiction is generally shelved separately, alphabetically by the author's last name. Large-print books, graphic novels, and books in languages other than the dominant local language are usually given their own sections.

The reference section is positioned near staff workstations and holds materials that do not circulate, such as encyclopedias, atlases, and local history collections. Specialized reference librarians staff a separate desk in larger libraries.

A children's room is typically located in a part of the building where noise will not disturb adult readers. It contains picture books, early readers, juvenile fiction, and juvenile nonfiction, along with low shelving and small furniture. A separate young-adult area, for older children and teenagers, may be adjacent.

Computer terminals for public use are clustered in a designated computer area or scattered along the perimeter of the main floor. The terminals provide access to the library's online catalog, to subscription databases, and to the general internet. Wireless internet access is also typically available throughout the building.

Quiet study rooms, group meeting rooms, a periodicals area for current newspapers and magazines, and a small café or vending area may also be present, depending on the size and budget of the library. Larger libraries often include a public meeting hall, an archives or local history room, and dedicated areas for community programs such as story times, computer classes, and book clubs.

The overall layout aims to balance accessibility, quiet, and the needs of different user groups within a single shared space.
